# Research Agenda

## v1 focus

The first research agenda is climate volatility broadly understood.

The goal is not to predict a single future state. The goal is to test whether meta-autoresearch can support a more useful exploration of scenario space in a domain where historical continuity is weakening and premature closure remains common.

## Primary questions

### 1. Can the method be operationalized without collapsing into vagueness?

The project begins with a philosophical posture. v1 must determine whether that posture can become a repeatable workflow with explicit outputs, constraints, and evaluation criteria.

### 2. What counts as a stable measurement in this kind of inquiry?

Machine learning has validation loss. This project does not. One of the central questions is how to construct a sufficiently stable evaluation framework for scenarios, syntheses, and research iterations.

### 3. Can scenario-space exploration improve climate thinking?

The working hypothesis is that scenario generation, recombination, and curation may reveal risks, interactions, and preparedness questions that linear prediction framing tends to miss.

### 4. What is the right division of labor between human judgment and AI generation?

The project needs to identify where AI is genuinely useful, where it introduces noise or false confidence, and where human curation must remain primary.

### 5. What artifacts does the method naturally produce?

It is still an open question whether the most valuable outputs are scenarios, field guides, synthesis documents, preparedness maps, research notes, or something else.

## Domain emphasis

The initial climate work should stay broad enough to avoid premature narrowing. Likely areas include:

- volatility and non-stationarity
- extreme weather clustering and compounding effects
- infrastructure and institutional fragility
- preparedness under uncertainty
- public misunderstanding caused by outdated baselines

## Near-term deliverables

- deepen the current `whiplash` and `breadbasket` branches without losing curation pressure
- compare branch variants more explicitly at the importer, reserve, and governance layers
- document what the first cycles reveal about recurring epistemic structure types
- sharpen the evaluation layer so branch maturity can be judged more consistently
- decide what counts as a strong enough cycle to be repeated later with cheaper or local models

## Exclusions for v1

To keep the scope disciplined, this phase will not aim to:

- produce authoritative forecasts
- build a production software platform
- solve climate communication in general
- generalize to many domains before the climate workflow is legible

## Evaluation questions for each iteration

Each substantial round of work should be tested against questions like:

- Did this expand the meaningful search space?
- Did it sharpen or muddy the evaluation criteria?
- Did the curation decisions remain explicit?
- Did the work surface a genuinely useful scenario or insight?
- Did it reduce false certainty, or merely multiply possibilities?

## What would count as progress

Progress in v1 would look like a repository that can reliably produce structured climate explorations, compare them honestly, and show how conclusions emerge without pretending to settle more than the process actually supports.

At the current stage, progress also means:

- proving that branch comparison is more informative than isolated scenario polishing
- distinguishing stronger-evidenced variants from more conceptually generative ones
- identifying whether the project is discovering reusable epistemic structure types rather than only accumulating climate content
