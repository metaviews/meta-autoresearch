## What was considered

This record makes explicit several weaker directions that were implicitly left behind as the first non-climate proving-ground branch became more structured.

## Direction 1: Generic inequality commentary

### Why it seemed promising

Wealth concentration is often discussed in moral and distributive terms, so starting with broad inequality language initially seemed like an easy and legitimate way to frame the branch.

### Why it was not kept as-is

- it described the problem without exposing the mechanism
- it did not distinguish between stock inequality and system dynamics
- it risked turning the branch into commentary rather than structural inquiry

### What changed the judgment

The branch became much more useful once it was reframed around balance-sheet compounding, asset-regime mismatch, and hidden transmission channels.

### Disposition

- discard: generic inequality commentary as a primary scenario form
- merge into: `research/scenarios/2026-03-28-wealth-concentration-structural-stress.md`
- hold for later: no
- conditions for revival: only as contextual framing around a more bounded mechanism

### Method lesson

Obviousness is not enough. A non-climate branch still needs hidden structure, not only a socially familiar problem statement.

## Direction 2: Single-cause tax explanation

### Why it seemed promising

Once the US asset-regime mismatch case appeared, it was tempting to let tax design explain the whole branch.

### Why it was not kept as-is

- it flattened the role of transmission, intermediation, and financial structure
- it risked turning the branch into a policy-advocacy claim instead of a comparative proving-ground
- it made the branch less useful for testing whether multiple structures were operative at once

### What changed the judgment

The India transmission case made it clear that the branch was not only about rule fit. A transmission challenger was genuinely present.

### Disposition

- discard: single-cause tax-design explanation as the whole branch
- merge into: `research/scenarios/2026-03-28-us-asset-regime-and-wealth-lock-in.md`
- hold for later: no
- conditions for revival: only if later evidence shows transmission channels add little explanatory value

### Method lesson

Bounded cases help, but they should not be mistaken too quickly for total branch explanations.

## Direction 3: Overgeneralized global-finance abstraction

### Why it seemed promising

The branch's transmission side naturally pointed toward large-scale global finance, private credit, and opaque balance-sheet channels, which made broad financial-system language feel attractive.

### Why it was not kept as-is

- it risked staying too abstract to compare meaningfully with the rule-conflict case
- it did not show clearly enough how downstream resilience was affected
- it could have reproduced a vague systemic-risk narrative without a bounded proving-ground case

### What changed the judgment

The India NBFC/MFI case made the transmission side much more concrete by showing how downstream borrowers and smaller lenders were squeezed.

### Disposition

- discard: broad transmission framing as a sufficient end state
- merge into: `research/scenarios/2026-03-28-private-credit-resilience-squeeze.md`
- hold for later: no
- conditions for revival: only as a parent framing above more bounded transmission cases

### Method lesson

Transmission arguments need downstream specificity or they remain too easy to agree with abstractly.

## Overall method lesson

The wealth-concentration branch improved when it stopped treating familiarity as clarity. Its strongest progress came from forcing visible comparison between bounded rule-conflict and bounded transmission cases rather than settling for either generic inequality language or one elegant explanatory story.
