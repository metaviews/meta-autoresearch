## What was considered

This record makes explicit several hydrologic directions that were left behind as the branch matured from an abstract water-stress idea into a more specific design-and-rule conflict branch.

## Direction 1: Generic water-crisis framing

### Why it seemed promising

"Water crisis" language was initially attractive because it is broad, urgent, and easy to connect to climate volatility.

### Why it was not kept as-is

- it does not explain what kind of hydrologic failure is actually happening
- it blurs infrastructure, governance, allocation, and weather into one bucket
- it does not give the branch enough mechanism to compare cases productively

### What changed the judgment

The branch became far more useful once it focused on design assumptions, operating thresholds, and rule conflicts rather than generalized crisis language.

### Disposition

- discard: generic water-crisis framing as a primary branch form
- merge into: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-hydrologic-whiplash-and-design-failure.md`
- hold for later: no
- conditions for revival: only as broad context around a more specific rule or infrastructure mechanism

### Method lesson

Hydrologic stress becomes methodologically useful only when it is tied to a specific system design problem.

## Direction 2: Event-only flood storytelling

### Why it seemed promising

Large flood events are easy to narrate and provide immediate drama, especially in cases like South East Queensland.

### Why it was not kept as-is

- event narrative alone does not show why the branch is about design conflict rather than weather severity
- it encourages forensic blame stories over structural analysis
- it makes comparison with non-flood-centered cases much harder

### What changed the judgment

The SEQ case became stronger only when the branch focused on drought-security design, grid logic, and dual-purpose reservoir operations, not on the flood event alone.

### Disposition

- discard: flood-event storytelling without explicit infrastructure or operating-rule tension
- merge into: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-seq-grid-and-wivenhoe-whiplash.md`
- hold for later: no
- conditions for revival: only if a future case uses event chronology to illuminate a clearly bounded design-rule conflict

### Method lesson

Narrative intensity is not enough; hydrologic branches need rule or infrastructure specificity to become comparable.

## Direction 3: Ordinary policy-conflict interpretation

### Why it seemed promising

Especially in the Upper Colorado case, it is tempting to treat the problem as ordinary political conflict over scarce water rather than as a more specific hydrologic design issue.

### Why it was not kept as-is

- it underweights the role of threshold-governed operations and inherited storage/allocation assumptions
- it flattens a design-stress signal into a generic governance story
- it would make the branch less useful for structural comparison with SEQ

### What changed the judgment

The branch became more distinctive once threshold ladders, emergency chronology, and redesign effort were treated as signals of rule mismatch under stress rather than as mere political noise.

### Disposition

- discard: generic policy-conflict framing as the main explanation of the Upper Colorado case
- merge into: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-upper-colorado-compact-and-storage-whiplash.md`
- hold for later: no
- conditions for revival: only if later evidence shows design mismatch adds little explanatory value beyond ordinary policy conflict

### Method lesson

Some governance stories become methodologically important only when they are recognized as rule-stress stories rather than generic political disagreement.

## Overall method lesson

The hydrologic branch improved once it stopped asking only where water stress appears and started asking where inherited design logic is no longer a good fit for the system it is trying to govern.
