# SEQ Grid and Wivenhoe Whiplash

## Metadata

- title: SEQ Grid and Wivenhoe Whiplash
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: regional variant
- domain slice: hydrologic volatility, urban water systems, drought portfolio design, flood operations

## Research question

How might a networked urban water system such as South East Queensland experience preparedness failure when drought-driven water-security design and flood-mitigation operations are both rational on their own terms, but become fragile when extreme wet and dry conditions arrive in faster and more uneven sequence than the system was built to hold comfortably?

## Why this scenario exists

The parent hydrologic branch is strong conceptually but under-grounded. South East Queensland is the first named variant because it provides a visible sequence: long drought pressure pushed the region toward portfolio diversification and grid-based water security, then the 2010-11 wet/flood period exposed how the same system also depends on difficult dual-purpose operating rules and flood-space tradeoffs.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - South East Queensland is a climate-of-extremes planning region with both drought and flood stress
  - the SEQ Water Grid and associated portfolio assets were shaped by serious drought-era water security concerns
  - Wivenhoe's dual role in water supply and flood mitigation creates real operating tensions under extreme inflow events
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the main failure is not a single flood event or a single drought event, but the fact that one system must absorb both scarcity-oriented and flood-oriented logics over a volatile period
  - diversified infrastructure can increase resilience while still leaving the system institutionally brittle at operating-rule boundaries
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a stress-test scenario about system design and operational logic, not a forensic claim about one event's cause
  - the focus is on planning and infrastructure interaction rather than complete hydrologic reconstruction

## Scenario logic

South East Queensland spends years oriented toward water security under drought pressure. The response is not passive: the region builds a more diversified and networked system, including grid interconnection, desalination, recycled-water capacity, and trigger-based drought planning. In one sense, this is a resilience success.

Then the hydroclimate shifts hard. Seqwater's own planning materials tie the water-grid buildout to the Millennium Drought (`2001-2009`), while the Bureau of Meteorology records 2010 as Queensland's wettest year on record, with the wettest spring and wettest December on record before the January 2011 flood sequence. Wet conditions and extreme rainfall arrive at a scale that changes the operating problem. The system is no longer mainly concerned with how to keep enough water in storage, but with how to manage flood-space, inflows, downstream risk, and a dual-purpose reservoir under extreme conditions. The infrastructure is not simply overwhelmed by weather. It is stressed because its operating logic now has to move between two different planning worlds.

The hydrologic whiplash lies in that transition. A region designed around scarcity management and portfolio reliability must rapidly operate as a flood-mitigation system under one of the wettest periods in state history. The public story can treat these as separate eras. The operational system cannot. The same institutions, assets, and rules have to absorb both.

The preparedness failure is therefore not that South East Queensland lacked infrastructure. It is that even a diversified infrastructure system can remain brittle when volatility exposes tensions among design assumptions, regional imbalance, trigger-based planning, and dual-purpose operating rules. Seqwater's own materials sharpen this further: flood operations must balance dam safety, downstream flooding, environmental impacts, transport/property disruption, and restoring full drinking-water supply, while forecast-only prereleases below full supply level are constrained and about half of the Brisbane catchment lies below the dams, limiting what operators can control.

## Grounding

- South East Queensland's water planning was reshaped by drought-era security concerns and a portfolio approach rather than one big-dam logic.
- Seqwater publicly frames the region as subject to a climate of extremes and documents trigger-based drought responses.
- The SEQ Water Grid exists partly because rainfall can be patchy and storage conditions uneven across the region.
- Queensland's 2010 and early 2011 wet period was extreme enough to produce one of the state's most significant flood sequences.
- Wivenhoe Dam is explicitly managed for both water supply and flood mitigation, making the case ideal for a design-and-rule conflict analysis.
- Seqwater's drought and flood planning materials now make the chronology and constraints clearer: drought-era portfolio buildout, 2009 desal activation, 2010 climatic reversal, and explicit multi-objective flood-operations rules.

These sources do not prove a singular design failure. They do support this as a strong first case for the hydrologic branch because the region visibly moves between scarcity logic and flood logic inside one integrated system.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - public framing of South East Queensland as a climate-of-extremes region
  - drought-triggered portfolio design and networked water transfers
  - severe flood-period operational pressure in the same broader system
  - documented multi-objective rule constraints around flood operations and water-supply restoration
- evidence classes consulted:
  - Bureau of Meteorology climate summaries
  - Seqwater system, drought-plan, and operating-manual materials
  - public planning-review documentation
- missing evidence:
  - more direct public or operator language on how the transition stressed decision-making beyond current planning documents
  - a clearer comparison with another hydrologic case that is less urban-grid oriented

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: high; the region is well matched to the branch mechanism and publicly documented
- internal coherence: high; the design-and-operations tension is clearer here than in the abstract parent scenario
- relevance: high; it directly tests hydrologic volatility as a planning and infrastructure problem
- preparedness value: high; it suggests stress-testing portfolio design and dual-purpose operations under volatile transitions
- novelty: high; it reframes a familiar drought/flood story as one systems-design problem rather than two separate eras
- status-quo challenge: high; it challenges the assumption that diversified infrastructure alone resolves volatility risk
- imaginative power: medium-high; it expands the hydrologic branch while staying tied to a documented operating context

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how much the central failure lies in operating rules versus broader climatic extremity
  - how generalizable this case is beyond highly networked urban water systems
  - how much the region's resilience portfolio reduced risk even while leaving other fragilities exposed
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply that drought-oriented investments caused flood vulnerability rather than coexisting with a different class of risk
  - it could oversimplify a complex and politically sensitive flood-operating context
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that the drought-flood sequence did not materially stress institutional transitions or planning assumptions
  - evidence that the central lesson is only event management, not hydrologic and design volatility
