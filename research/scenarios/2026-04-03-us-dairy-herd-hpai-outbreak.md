## Research question

How might HPAI H5N1 infection of US dairy herds create agricultural disruption not mainly through culling of infected animals, but through trade restriction amplification, milk supply chain disruption, and cross-state transmission that exposes the limitations of dairy sector biosecurity designed for bacterial threats rather than viral spillover?

## Why this scenario exists

This variant grounds the HPAI branch in a specific, ongoing named case. US dairy herd infections (2024-2025) represent a fundamentally new transmission pathway: cattle-to-cattle and cattle-to-human. This tests whether HPAI disruption follows correlation/transmission structure (like breadbasket) or a different mechanism.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - HPAI H5N1 detected in US dairy herds across multiple states (2024-2025)
  - Cattle-to-cattle transmission documented
  - First cattle-to-human transmission documented (US dairy worker, 2024)
  - US is world's largest dairy producer ($900B+ global industry)
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - dairy biosecurity designed for bacterial threats (brucellosis, TB) may be inadequate for viral spillover
  - trade restrictions on dairy exports could cascade through global dairy supply chains
  - cattle-to-cattle transmission may become self-sustaining without intervention
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a bounded stress test, not a pandemic forecast
  - the focus is on dairy sector disruption and trade amplification, not human mortality

## Scenario logic

HPAI H5N1 enters US dairy herds through wild bird contamination of feed or water. Infected cows show reduced milk production, fever, and thickened colostrum. The virus spreads between herds through cattle movement, shared equipment, or contaminated feed.

State and federal responses include testing requirements, movement restrictions, and culling of severely affected animals. Trading partners impose restrictions on US dairy exports. The disruption cascades: reduced milk production affects dairy product prices, feed grain demand shifts, and export-dependent economies face secondary disruption.

The preparedness failure lies in dairy biosecurity designed for bacterial containment facing a viral pathogen with wild bird reservoir. Unlike bacterial threats that can be contained through testing and culling, viral spillover from wild birds creates ongoing reintroduction risk that containment alone cannot address.

## Grounding

- USDA APHIS confirmed HPAI H5N1 in US dairy herds (March 2024)
- Cases detected in multiple states: Texas, Kansas, Colorado, Michigan, Idaho, others
- First human case: Texas dairy worker (April 2024), mild conjunctivitis
- PB2 E627K mutation detected in cattle isolates (mammalian adaptation signal)
- USDA implemented testing requirements for interstate cattle movement
- Trade restrictions imposed by some countries on US dairy products

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - is cattle-to-cattle transmission self-sustaining or requires repeated wild bird spillover?
  - how effective are current biosecurity measures for viral containment?
  - will trade restrictions escalate or remain proportionate?
- where this could be misleading:
  - could overstate disruption if cattle transmission is self-limiting
  - could understate if virus adapts to more efficient cattle-to-cattle transmission
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that current biosecurity effectively contains viral spread
  - evidence that trade restrictions are targeted and non-cascading
