# US Asset-Regime and Wealth Lock-In

## Metadata

- title: US Asset-Regime and Wealth Lock-In
- date: 2026-03-28
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: grounded variant
- domain slice: political economy, tax design, asset structure, intergenerational compounding

## Research question

How might wealth concentration persist and intensify because the tax and asset regime maps more closely to housing-heavy middle wealth than to the financial and business asset structures through which top wealth compounds?

## Why this scenario exists

The parent wealth-concentration branch is currently hybrid. This grounded US variant tests whether `design/rule conflict under volatility` is one of its strongest concrete channels. It does this by focusing on a mismatch between rule architecture and asset architecture rather than trying to explain all wealth concentration at once.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - wealth concentration is strongly shaped by institutional design, not only by market luck
  - top wealth holders are more concentrated in business and financial assets than middle households
  - tax and transfer rules matter for how long gains can compound before meaningful taxation occurs
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the key issue is not simply low taxation, but poor rule fit with the main compounding channels of concentrated wealth
  - systems can appear neutral while structurally targeting the more visible and less scalable assets of the middle class more consistently than top-heavy financial wealth
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a US institutional stress test, not a total theory of global wealth concentration
  - the scenario focuses on asset form, basis, realization, and transfer rules rather than every channel of inequality

## Scenario logic

The system taxes and regulates wealth through categories that fit some forms of ownership better than others. Housing and property are visible, locally anchored, and repeatedly taxable. Large financial and business asset pools are more scalable, more deferable, and more easily positioned for long compounding runs. The result is not that top wealth faces no rules. The result is that the rules can fit its balance-sheet structure less directly.

As top wealth becomes increasingly financial and business-heavy, while middle balance sheets remain housing-heavy, a mismatch grows between the visible objects of taxation and the main channels of compounding. Realization-based taxation and inherited-basis rules can further defer when appreciation becomes taxable, while estate-tax application remains limited to high thresholds. In that context, wealth concentration is not just a matter of who earns more. It is a matter of how the legal and tax architecture shapes the timing, visibility, and incidence of compounding.

The preparedness failure lies in continuing to argue about inequality as if the system were mainly failing at redistribution after the fact. If the architecture is mismatched to the actual forms through which wealth compounds, then the system is reproducing concentration upstream through design fit, not only tolerating it downstream.

## Grounding

- WID shows that top wealth is much more financial-asset-heavy while middle wealth is much more housing-heavy
- WID states that most existing wealth taxation still falls mainly through property and inheritance systems
- the Federal Reserve shows that middle family balance sheets are dominated by housing while business ownership is far more common in top-income families
- IRS materials show how basis and inherited-basis rules determine gain recognition and how estate taxation applies at transfer with high thresholds

These sources support a grounded variant in which current rules appear increasingly misaligned with the balance-sheet forms through which concentrated wealth grows.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - asset-structure asymmetry between middle and top wealth
  - property-heavy wealth taxation
  - realization and transfer rules that affect appreciation timing
  - increasing concentration of gains at the top
- evidence classes consulted:
  - WID structural and policy analysis
  - Federal Reserve balance-sheet survey material
  - IRS explanatory materials on basis and estate treatment
- missing evidence:
  - stronger direct evidence linking these rule mismatches to measured concentration dynamics in a bounded US case
  - a clearer comparison to another national regime
  - more explicit evidence on how business equity and financial claims are taxed in effective practice across the top tail

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how much of the observed concentration dynamic this specific mismatch explains relative to other mechanisms
  - whether the US case is the best proving-ground example for this institutional pathway
  - how much effective tax incidence differs from formal rule structure in practice
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply that top wealth is untaxed rather than differently and often less directly targeted
  - it could overstate the universality of the US pattern
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that the asset-regime mismatch adds little explanatory value beyond standard inequality accounts
  - evidence that another mechanism, such as hidden intermediation, is more structurally central
