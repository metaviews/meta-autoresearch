# Northern Wheat Correlation Shock

## Metadata

- title: Northern Wheat Correlation Shock
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: regional/crop variant
- domain slice: climate volatility, wheat systems, trade transmission, import dependence

## Research question

How might correlated hot-dry stress across major northern wheat regions create a preparedness failure not because global wheat disappears, but because a major export node is impaired while substitute production regions are also weakened?

## Why this scenario exists

The parent `synchronous breadbasket stress` scenario is strong at the family level but still broad. This variant makes the family more operational by focusing on wheat, a crop with strong climate-signal support in the repo, and by specifying a transmission chain: correlated stress -> export restriction risk -> tighter stocks -> price and procurement stress in import-dependent regions.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - global wheat security depends heavily on a limited set of major production and export regions
  - price and procurement stress can become serious before any notion of global wheat collapse is relevant
  - import-dependent regions are exposed not only to production shocks but to export policy and market reactions
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the preparedness failure comes from reduced substitutability across wheat regions, not from a single failed harvest alone
  - policy amplification is part of the climate-risk pathway rather than an external add-on
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a stress-test scenario built around wheat systems, not a forecast of one specific year
  - Russia is used as a transmission anchor and historical analog, while China is used mainly as a production-buffer stress node rather than an export node

## Scenario logic

A given growing season produces hot-dry stress across major northern wheat regions. Russia experiences enough production pressure to raise export concern, while another large wheat region such as China also underperforms, reducing the system's ability to compensate through substitution. The absolute production loss is serious but not globally terminal. The system problem emerges because the remaining slack is thinner than institutions assumed.

As revised harvest expectations arrive, market anxiety intensifies. A Russian-style export restriction or credible fear of one narrows trade confidence further. Import-dependent wheat buyers, especially in MENA markets, face tighter procurement conditions and price spikes. Humanitarian and state buyers discover that reserve assumptions and sourcing plans built around partial regional failure do not hold as well under correlated stress.

The preparedness failure is not simply that wheat yields fall. It is that climate correlation removes fallback options at the same moment that policy responses and stock tightness make trade less forgiving.

## Grounding

- Heino et al. (2023) support wheat as the strongest first crop target for compound hot-dry stress, including larger losses in relatively cool regions such as Russia and China.
- Gaupp et al. (2020) support the broader claim that simultaneous breadbasket failure risk is rising and relevant to reserve planning.
- The 2010 Russia flash drought case provides a concrete historical analog for the transmission mechanism from crop stress to export restriction and price escalation.
- Middle East wheat-price sensitivity research supports the idea that downstream impact is highly uneven and teleconnected.

These sources do not prove that the next major wheat shock will unfold exactly this way. They do support this as a disciplined first variant of the breadbasket family.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - increasing probability of compound hot-dry stress in wheat-growing regions
  - historical evidence that Russian wheat shocks can amplify internationally through export policy and prices
  - high downstream sensitivity in wheat-import-dependent regions
- evidence classes consulted:
  - crop-yield and compound-extreme literature
  - simultaneous breadbasket failure literature
  - trade and price transmission studies
  - export-ban policy analysis
- missing evidence:
  - stronger open-access evidence tying reserve adequacy directly to correlated wheat stress
  - more detailed mapping of which MENA states or buyers are most exposed under this exact variant
  - a clearer comparison between China, Europe, and North America as the second stress node

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: high; the climate and transmission mechanisms are both supported by the current evidence base
- internal coherence: high; the scenario is more bounded and testable than the parent family
- relevance: high; it directly addresses preparedness for global food-system stress under volatility
- preparedness value: high; it points toward reserves, sourcing diversity, export-risk planning, and import exposure
- novelty: high; it reframes wheat shock as a correlation-and-policy problem rather than a single-country crop failure
- status-quo challenge: high; it challenges the assumption that global grain markets will absorb regional wheat shocks smoothly
- imaginative power: medium-high; it expands the family into a more operational trade-and-procurement frame without drifting from the evidence

## Curation notes

- current curation gate:
  - keep
- why keep this scenario:
  - it gives the breadbasket family its first concrete subcase with a legible transmission pathway
  - it builds directly on the strongest climate evidence already in the repo
  - it offers a good bridge from physical climate stress to actual preparedness questions
- what should be refined next:
  - compare the selected Russia-Europe trade-buffer variant against a later Russia-China production-buffer variant
  - add more explicit reserve and procurement implications
  - test an alternate version where export coordination succeeds instead of failing
- what might cause this scenario to be revised or merged:
  - if the China-plus-Russia framing proves less useful than a Russia-plus-Europe or Russia-plus-North-America variant
  - if the scenario works better as part of a broader wheat-trade family than as a stand-alone variant

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - which second major wheat region provides the clearest real-world comparison node
  - how much price escalation depends on policy response versus physical stress alone
  - how resilient current reserves and alternative sourcing channels actually are
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply climate is the sole driver of price or procurement stress
  - it could overstate Russia-China symmetry when their trade roles differ substantially
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that wheat trade and reserve systems consistently absorb major exporter shocks even under correlated stress
  - evidence that another crop or region pair is substantially more decision-useful than wheat for first deepening
