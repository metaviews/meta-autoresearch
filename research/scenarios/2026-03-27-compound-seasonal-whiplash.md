# Compound Seasonal Whiplash

## Metadata

- title: Compound Seasonal Whiplash
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: revised
- scenario type: stress-test oriented
- domain slice: climate volatility, institutional sequencing failure, regional preparedness

## Research question

How might increasing climate volatility produce a preparedness failure through a wet-to-hot-dry seasonal transition that institutions still treat as separate planning problems?

## Why this scenario exists

Many planning systems are built around stable seasonal categories: wet season, fire season, heat season, flood season, planting season, cooling season. This scenario is worth revising rather than discarding because the real failure may come from those categories breaking down faster than planning systems do. The key adjustment in this revision is to narrow the scenario to a more grounded sequence: a wet period that creates temporary relief and hidden exposure, followed by a hot-dry turn that reveals how badly systems misread the transition.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - climate non-stationarity is already reducing the reliability of historical baselines in some regions
  - institutional planning cycles remain anchored to older seasonal expectations
  - infrastructure and public communication are often optimized for isolated hazards rather than compounding sequences
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the main disruption is not one record event but a chain of seasonal transitions that interact
  - temporary relief after a wet period can actively mislead planners if it obscures later hot-dry risk
  - the highest preparedness value may come from scenario chains rather than event-specific forecasts
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a bounded scenario family for regions where wet-period growth, flood stress, or storage relief can be followed by hotter and drier conditions in the same extended season or planning cycle
  - the scenario focuses on institutional and social consequences more than detailed physical climate modeling
  - it is not a claim that every region will experience this sequence in the same way

## Scenario logic

A region enters a year with a wetter-than-expected period that appears, at first, to reduce climate stress. Reservoirs recover somewhat, vegetation surges, and public attention shifts away from scarcity. The same period also leaves hidden liabilities behind: higher fuel loads, saturated soils, deferred infrastructure maintenance, crop timing changes, and administrative exhaustion from flood or storm response.

The pattern then flips. The following phase arrives hotter and drier than planning norms assume. Landscapes recently framed in terms of relief now become fire-prone or water-stressed. Water managers, grid operators, public-health agencies, insurers, schools, and local governments are forced to shift operating assumptions midstream. Public messaging lags because the institutional story is still linear: first flood, then drought, then fire, each treated as a separate issue instead of as one compounding sequence.

The real disruption comes from sequencing. Households and agencies spend resources responding to the early phase, then enter the later phase with reduced margin. Agricultural planning becomes less reliable, cooling demand rises after infrastructure has already absorbed wet-period impacts, water quality and storage management become harder, air quality worsens during heat stress, and emergency systems face overlapping rather than isolated pressures.

The scenario does not require a single unprecedented event. It requires a sufficiently unstable transition that turns existing planning categories into a liability.

## Grounding and candidate contexts

This revision narrows the scenario to contexts where the sequence is most plausible and most decision-relevant.

- IPCC Working Group I assesses that heavy precipitation has increased over most land areas with sufficient observations, that agricultural and ecological drought has increased in some regions, and that human influence has likely increased the chance of compound extreme events including concurrent heatwaves and droughts.
- IPCC Working Group II assesses that complex risks result from multiple hazards occurring concurrently or interacting across systems, that observed increases in extremes have already affected settlements and infrastructure, and that key infrastructure systems become more vulnerable when design standards do not account for changing climate conditions.
- The current repo evidence base also supports treating hot-dry stress as a major mechanism across food and land systems, which strengthens the plausibility of a wet-to-hot-dry transition becoming operationally important even when the exact local sequence varies.

Most promising candidate contexts for follow-up variants:

- Mediterranean-climate or fire-prone regions where wet growth seasons can raise later fuel risk
- water systems that experience flood-management and drought-management as administratively separate worlds
- urban or peri-urban regions where infrastructure, heat stress, smoke, and water strain can stack after a wet-period response phase

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - increasing discussion of non-stationarity in climate and risk planning
  - growing incidence of compound and cascading hazards in public reporting and research
  - institutional difficulty communicating climate conditions that shift faster than seasonal narratives
  - assessment evidence that interacting hazards and infrastructure vulnerability are already increasing
- evidence classes consulted:
  - IPCC Working Group I and II summaries
  - climate volatility and non-stationarity literature
  - reporting and literature on compound hazards, fire weather, flooding, heat, and infrastructure stress
  - preparedness and resilience planning literature
- missing evidence:
  - region-specific case studies showing how wet-period relief followed by hot-dry stress degraded decision quality
  - stronger examples of resource exhaustion across hazard sequences rather than single events
  - concrete planning examples where hazard categories were managed separately and failed as a result

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: moderate; the mechanism is plausible and better bounded than before, but still needs case-specific support
- internal coherence: high; the institutional lag mechanism is clear and does not depend on a single extreme claim
- relevance: high; it directly tests how preparedness fails under volatility rather than under isolated events
- preparedness value: high; it suggests planning for transitions and chains, not just peak events
- novelty: high; it directly challenges the habit of treating seasonal hazards as separate planning domains
- status-quo challenge: high; it pressures default seasonal categories and isolated-hazard planning
- imaginative power: high; it opens a useful line of inquiry around sequence blindness without leaving disciplined bounds

## Curation notes

- current curation gate:
  - revise
- why keep this scenario in revised form:
  - it points toward a real planning problem: sequence blindness
  - it challenges a status-quo planning frame that still treats hazards as isolated domains
  - it can generate concrete follow-up questions for infrastructure, agriculture, public health, and communication
- what should be refined next:
  - add one or two region-specific variants tied to named climate and governance contexts
  - identify published case studies that resemble the wet-to-hot-dry transition logic
  - test whether the scenario is more useful when framed as institutional lag rather than climate hazard alone
- what might cause this scenario to be discarded or merged:
  - if it still proves too generic to distinguish useful subtypes after grounding attempts
  - if a narrower compound-risk or infrastructure-sequencing scenario captures the mechanism more clearly

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how common or regionally specific this wet-to-hot-dry sequence pattern is
  - whether institutions fail mainly because of volatility itself or because of existing underinvestment and fragmentation
  - how much the scenario depends on public communication lag versus hard infrastructure limits
- where this could be misleading:
  - it may still bundle too many hazards into one narrative without enough empirical constraint
  - it may understate the importance of local geography and governance differences
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that multi-hazard planning systems already handle these transitions better than assumed
  - evidence that event-specific planning remains more decision-useful than sequence-based planning
