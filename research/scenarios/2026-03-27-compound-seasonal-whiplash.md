# Compound Seasonal Whiplash

## Metadata

- title: Compound Seasonal Whiplash
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: exploratory
- domain slice: climate volatility, preparedness, institutional planning

## Research question

How might increasing climate volatility produce a preparedness failure not through a single catastrophic event, but through rapid swings between seasonal conditions that institutions still treat as separate problems?

## Why this scenario exists

Many planning systems are built around stable seasonal categories: wet season, fire season, heat season, flood season, planting season, cooling season. A volatility-driven scenario is worth keeping because the real failure may come from those categories breaking down faster than planning systems do.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - climate non-stationarity is already reducing the reliability of historical baselines in some regions
  - institutional planning cycles remain anchored to older seasonal expectations
  - infrastructure and public communication are often optimized for isolated hazards rather than compounding sequences
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the main disruption is not one record event but a chain of atypical swings
  - the highest preparedness value may come from scenario chains rather than event-specific forecasts
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a broad, cross-region preparedness scenario rather than a claim about one named geography
  - the scenario focuses on institutional and social consequences more than physical climate modeling detail

## Scenario logic

A region enters a year with an unusually warm winter and an early biological start to the season. This is followed by a wet spring that boosts vegetation growth, fills some reservoirs, and creates a temporary sense of relief. The same conditions also increase fuel loads, pest activity, and infrastructure strain from repeated storm damage in places that are not expecting prolonged oscillation.

The pattern then flips. Summer arrives hotter and drier than public memory expects. The same landscapes that were recently discussed in terms of abundance become fire-prone. Water managers, grid operators, insurers, hospitals, schools, and local governments are forced to shift operating assumptions midstream. Public messaging lags because the institutional story is still linear: first flood, then drought, then fire, each treated as a separate issue.

The real disruption comes from sequencing. Households and agencies spend resources responding to the early phase, then enter the later phase with reduced margin. Agricultural planning becomes less reliable, cooling demand rises after infrastructure has already absorbed storm impacts, air quality worsens during heat stress, and emergency systems face overlapping rather than isolated pressures.

The scenario does not require a single unprecedented event. It requires a sufficiently unstable sequence that turns existing planning categories into a liability.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - increasing discussion of non-stationarity in climate and risk planning
  - growing incidence of compound and cascading hazards in public reporting and research
  - institutional difficulty communicating climate conditions that shift faster than seasonal narratives
- evidence classes consulted:
  - climate volatility and non-stationarity literature
  - reporting on compound hazards, fire-weather interactions, flooding, heat, and infrastructure stress
  - preparedness and resilience planning literature
- missing evidence:
  - region-specific case studies showing how rapid seasonal swings degrade decision quality
  - stronger examples of resource exhaustion across hazard sequences rather than single events

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: medium-high; the components are familiar, though the exact sequencing needs empirical grounding
- internal coherence: high; the institutional lag mechanism is clear and does not depend on a single extreme claim
- relevance: high; it directly tests how preparedness fails under volatility rather than under isolated events
- preparedness value: high; it suggests planning for transitions and chains, not just peak events
- novelty: medium; compound hazards are known, but the framing around seasonal category failure may be especially useful

## Curation notes

- why keep this scenario:
  - it is broad enough to apply across multiple regions without becoming empty
  - it points toward a real planning problem: sequence blindness
  - it can generate concrete follow-up questions for infrastructure, agriculture, public health, and communication
- what should be refined next:
  - add one or two region-specific variants
  - identify published case studies that resemble the sequence
  - test whether the scenario is more useful when framed as institutional lag rather than climate hazard alone
- what might cause this scenario to be discarded or merged:
  - if it proves too generic to distinguish useful subtypes
  - if a narrower compound-risk scenario captures the mechanism more clearly

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how common or regionally specific this sequence pattern is
  - whether institutions fail mainly because of volatility itself or because of existing underinvestment and fragmentation
  - how much the scenario depends on public communication lag versus hard infrastructure limits
- where this could be misleading:
  - it may bundle too many hazards into one narrative without enough empirical constraint
  - it may understate the importance of local geography and governance differences
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that multi-hazard planning systems already handle these transitions better than assumed
  - evidence that event-specific planning remains more decision-useful than sequence-based planning
