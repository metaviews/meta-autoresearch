## Research question

How might HPAI H5N1 spread through wild bird migration pathways create sequential agricultural disruption across continents, where the sequence of events (wild bird outbreak → poultry spillover → mammalian adaptation → agricultural disruption) creates failure modes that independent planning for each stage cannot address?

## Why this scenario exists

This variant grounds the HPAI branch in the wild bird migration pathway—the original source of global spread. It tests whether HPAI disruption follows sequence failure structure (like whiplash), where the chain of events matters more than any single stage.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - Wild bird migration spreads HPAI across continents seasonally
  - H5N1 is now enzootic in wild bird populations globally
  - Poultry operations in migration paths face recurring outbreak risk
  - Each outbreak creates opportunity for mammalian adaptation
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - eradication is impossible with current tools because wild bird reservoir cannot be eliminated
  - the sequence of transmission (wild bird → poultry → mammal) creates compounding risk at each stage
  - planning for each stage independently misses the sequential amplification
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a bounded stress test, not a prediction of specific migration patterns
  - the focus is on sequential transmission chain, not specific outbreak locations

## Scenario logic

Wild birds carrying HPAI migrate along established flyways (Atlantic, Pacific, Central, East Asian-Australasian). During migration, infected birds contaminate water sources, feed, and environments used by poultry operations. Poultry outbreaks require mass culling, but the virus persists in wild bird populations and recontaminates the environment.

Each poultry outbreak creates opportunity for viral spillover into mammals: farm workers, domestic animals, wildlife. Each spillover event is a lottery ticket for mammalian adaptation. Most fail, but the cumulative probability increases with each outbreak.

The preparedness failure lies in planning for each stage independently: wild bird surveillance, poultry biosecurity, mammalian monitoring. The sequence—how wild bird outbreaks create poultry risk, which creates mammalian adaptation opportunity—is not planned as a chain. Each stage is treated as a separate problem with separate protocols, separate agencies, separate funding.

## Grounding

- HPAI detected in wild birds across all major flyways (2020-2026)
- Atlantic flyway: outbreaks in North America, Europe, Africa
- Pacific flyway: outbreaks in Asia, Australia, Pacific islands
- East Asian-Australasian flyway: highest wild bird diversity, highest outbreak frequency
- Poultry spillover documented in every continent with wild bird outbreaks
- Mammalian spillover documented: sea lions (South America), cattle (North America), foxes, bears, cats

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - what is the probability of mammalian adaptation per spillover event?
  - can wild bird reservoir be managed with current surveillance tools?
  - are there intervention points in the sequence that could break the chain?
- where this could be misleading:
  - could overstate sequential risk if stages are more independent than assumed
  - could understate if mammalian adaptation probability is higher than current estimates
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that wild bird surveillance effectively predicts and prevents poultry spillover
  - evidence that sequential stages are already coordinated in practice
