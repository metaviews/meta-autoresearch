# India Last-Mile Credit Squeeze

## Metadata

- title: India Last-Mile Credit Squeeze
- date: 2026-03-28
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: grounded variant
- domain slice: political economy, non-bank finance, wholesale funding withdrawal, downstream credit access

## Research question

How might concentrated non-bank funding and sudden withdrawal of intermediation create a preparedness failure by cutting off last-mile credit channels for smaller firms and low-income borrowers before the broader system appears to be in open crisis?

## Why this scenario exists

The private-credit scenario established a transmission-oriented framing, but it remained somewhat abstract and globally generalized. This India case gives that framing a more concrete downstream channel: smaller NBFCs and MFIs losing funding access after a major non-bank shock, with effects on borrowers that rely on them for working capital or household resilience.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - non-bank lenders can become critical conduits for growth and last-mile credit delivery
  - wholesale funding withdrawal can change lending conditions quickly even without an immediate generalized crash
  - weaker intermediaries and weaker borrowers are often hit first
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the main fragility is not simply default at the top, but withdrawal of credit farther down the chain
  - concentrated funding structures matter because they shape who loses access when confidence tightens
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a bounded transmission scenario, not a total account of India's financial system
  - the focus is on NBFC/MFI funding withdrawal and downstream resilience, not on all macroeconomic drivers

## Scenario logic

Non-bank lenders grow into major conduits of credit expansion. Because they are not all equally positioned, funding concentration matters. When a major non-bank shock such as IL&FS destabilizes confidence, wholesale funding becomes harder to access. Stronger institutions may survive with more room. Smaller NBFCs and MFIs, which often serve the last mile, lose access sooner and more sharply.

The system can still appear partly stable from above. But farther down, the withdrawal of intermediation begins to squeeze resilience. Micro borrowers lose a safety-net channel. Smaller firms find refinancing and working-capital support harder to obtain. The fragility is therefore transmitted not mainly through visible collapse, but through shrinking support where credit dependence is high and buffers are weak.

The preparedness failure lies in reading concentration only at the level of wealth stocks or top-level ownership while missing how concentrated funding channels can shape resilience for actors much farther from the top of the balance sheet hierarchy.

## Grounding

- IMF states that NBFCs drove about half of lending growth during 2015-17 and that funding conditions tightened sharply after IL&FS and DHFL
- IMF also reports a nearly `65%` reduction in mutual-fund exposure to NBFC commercial paper since September 2018
- RBI states that smaller NBFCs and MFIs providing last-mile credit were especially squeezed
- RBI notes that microfinance serves `6 crore` borrowers and MSMEs are central to employment, GDP, and exports

These sources support a more concrete transmission variant in which intermediation withdrawal, not only top-level loss, becomes the key structural channel.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - large NBFC role in credit growth
  - abrupt wholesale-funding tightening after high-profile non-bank failures
  - stronger stress on smaller and more downstream lenders
  - large borrower populations dependent on those channels
- evidence classes consulted:
  - IMF system analysis
  - RBI speeches and policy-facing remarks
  - IGIDR working paper on credit slowdown
- missing evidence:
  - tighter borrower-level or sector-specific evidence on effects beyond credit availability
  - stronger comparative evidence against another country or sectoral case
  - more explicit ownership concentration links at the top of the funding chain

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: high; the transmission logic is concretely grounded
- internal coherence: high; this is a stronger downstream channel than the broader private-credit framing alone
- relevance: high; it materially strengthens the transmission side of the wealth-concentration branch
- preparedness value: medium-high; it shows how credit withdrawal can become a resilience issue before broader narratives catch up
- novelty: high; it shifts the branch toward last-mile fragility rather than only top-level financial structure
- status-quo challenge: high; it challenges the idea that concentration matters only where ownership is visibly concentrated
- imaginative power: medium-high; it narrows the branch in a productive way while deepening the transmission claim

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how much concentrated wealth versus general non-bank fragility explains the case
  - how far borrower-level effects can be documented beyond lender stress and credit slowdown
  - how portable the India case is to other settings
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply direct one-step causal links from elite wealth concentration to borrower outcomes that still need more intermediate evidence
  - it could overgeneralize from a specific institutional environment
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that the downstream squeeze was too specific or too temporary to support a broader transmission reading
  - evidence that the rule-conflict branch is still substantially more explanatory than this transmission branch
