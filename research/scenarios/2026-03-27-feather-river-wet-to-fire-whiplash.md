# Feather River Wet-to-Fire Whiplash

- title: Feather River Wet-to-Fire Whiplash

- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: regional variant
- domain slice: California climate volatility, water systems, wildfire preparedness, institutional sequencing failure

## Research question

How might a Northern Sierra planning geography such as the Feather River system experience a preparedness failure when a wet or recovery phase shifts institutional attention toward relief, only for later hot-dry and fire conditions to expose how separate hazard planning categories fail to connect?

## Why this scenario exists

The broader `compound seasonal whiplash` family became more useful after narrowing to a wet-to-hot-dry transition. This regional variant tests whether that mechanism becomes more legible when placed in a named geography where reservoirs, snowpack, vegetation growth, wildfire risk, transmission infrastructure, and emergency response all overlap.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - California already faces recognized swings between very dry and very wet conditions
  - wet periods in Northern California can produce partial storage relief and a public sense that immediate scarcity has eased
  - the Feather River corridor sits inside a region where major fire, water, and infrastructure systems interact
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the key failure is not simply a fire or a flood, but the transition between planning modes
  - institutional relief after wet conditions can become part of the vulnerability if later hot-dry conditions arrive before systems have adapted
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a stress-test scenario, not a reconstruction of one historical year
  - the scenario uses the Feather River region as a planning geography, not as a claim that every watershed process there behaves identically

## Scenario logic

The region exits a dry period into a wet season that improves reservoir conditions and softens immediate scarcity narratives. Water agencies regain some flexibility, the public reads the year as partial recovery, and political attention shifts away from chronic stress. At the same time, the wet period contributes to stronger vegetation growth, infrastructure wear from storms or runoff, and administrative fatigue from managing flood-space and storage tradeoffs.

The following hot-dry phase arrives before planning assumptions have truly changed. Fuels dry, fire danger rises, air quality risk increases, and power, water, transport, and emergency systems face simultaneous pressure. The same geography that was recently framed in terms of replenishment now has to operate in scarcity and emergency mode again.

The preparedness failure is not that no one knows wildfire or drought can happen. It is that the systems governing reservoirs, fire preparation, transmission, communications, and public expectations are still organized as if relief and danger belong to separate seasons with cleaner boundaries than the region now reliably provides.

## Grounding

- California Water Watch already presents California as managing swings between extreme dry and extreme wet conditions.
- California reservoir summaries show that Sacramento-region storage can move well above historical averages in wet periods, supporting the plausibility of a relief phase.
- CAL FIRE incident records show that the Feather River Canyon is also a site of severe wildfire exposure, including the Dixie Fire in 2021.

Taken together, these sources do not prove one simple deterministic chain. They do support the region as a plausible setting for a sequence-based stress test.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - California framing around hydrologic volatility and swing conditions
  - reservoir and water-management sensitivity to wet recovery narratives
  - severe fire exposure in the same broader planning geography
- evidence classes consulted:
  - California state water monitoring and storage summaries
  - CAL FIRE incident records
  - IPCC assessments on interacting hazards and infrastructure vulnerability
- missing evidence:
  - a tighter local chronology linking wet recovery conditions to later hot-dry operational strain
  - case material on how local agencies or communities interpreted the relief phase
  - clearer documentation of which planning systems failed to coordinate across the sequence

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: moderate; the regional fit is credible, but the causal chain still needs stronger local evidence
- internal coherence: high; the mechanism is clear and bounded
- relevance: high; it directly tests institutional sequencing failure in a named geography
- preparedness value: high; it suggests cross-hazard planning around transitions rather than isolated event response
- novelty: high; it reframes a familiar fire-and-water landscape as a sequence problem
- status-quo challenge: high; it challenges separate seasonal and sectoral planning assumptions
- imaginative power: medium-high; it is more bounded than the parent scenario but still expands how the region can be thought about

  - it offers a tractable next step for evidence gathering
  - it is easier to test and refine than the broader parent scenario
- what should be refined next:
  - identify a specific year or cluster of years that best fits the transition logic
  - map which systems are most sequence-sensitive: reservoirs, fuels, transmission, evacuation, or public communication
  - compare this case with another California or western North America geography
- what might cause this scenario to be revised or merged:
  - if the local evidence never supports the transition strongly enough
  - if the scenario works better as a subtype of a larger California hydroclimate whiplash family

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how directly wet-period conditions contribute to later fire vulnerability in this named case
  - how much of the risk comes from climate volatility versus infrastructure, governance, and maintenance failures
  - whether the Feather River corridor is the clearest first case compared with another California region
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply a single linear climate-causal story where the actual drivers are layered and partly independent
  - it could overstate how much short-term storage recovery changes institutional behavior without direct case evidence
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that planning agencies already coordinate well across wet-to-fire transitions in this geography
  - evidence that the best explanation of observed failures lies mostly outside the sequence logic
