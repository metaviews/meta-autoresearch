## Research question

How might HPAI H5N1 mass mortality in South American sea lions expose the mismatch between wildlife biosecurity frameworks designed for contained outbreaks and a pathogen with wild bird reservoir that creates ongoing, uncontrollable reintroduction risk?

## Why this scenario exists

This variant grounds the HPAI branch in a specific mass mortality event: 20,000+ South American sea lion deaths (Peru, Chile, 2023). It tests whether HPAI disruption follows design/rule conflict structure (like hydrologic), where biosecurity frameworks designed for one type of outbreak face fundamentally different pathogen dynamics.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - HPAI H5N1 caused mass mortality in South American sea lions (2023)
  - 20,000+ sea lions died across Peru and Chile coastlines
  - Mammal-to-mammal transmission documented
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - wildlife biosecurity frameworks assume contained outbreaks with identifiable source and containment endpoint
  - HPAI with wild bird reservoir creates ongoing reintroduction risk that containment cannot address
  - mass mortality of apex marine predators has cascading ecosystem effects
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a bounded stress test of wildlife biosecurity, not a marine ecology forecast
  - the focus is on design/rule mismatch, not ecosystem consequences

## Scenario logic

HPAI H5N1 reaches South American sea lions through contaminated prey (fish, seabirds) or direct contact with infected wild birds at haul-out sites. The virus spreads rapidly through dense sea lion colonies. Mortality is high: 20,000+ deaths across Peru and Chile coastlines.

Wildlife authorities respond with standard outbreak protocols: carcass removal, site quarantine, population monitoring. But the protocols are designed for contained outbreaks with identifiable source and endpoint. HPAI with wild bird reservoir creates ongoing reintroduction risk: wild birds continue to contaminate the environment, re-infecting sea lion colonies after apparent clearance.

The preparedness failure lies in wildlife biosecurity frameworks designed for contained outbreaks facing a pathogen with environmental reservoir that cannot be eliminated. Standard protocols—effective for rabies, distemper, or other contained wildlife diseases—fail when the pathogen is continuously reintroduced from wild bird populations.
