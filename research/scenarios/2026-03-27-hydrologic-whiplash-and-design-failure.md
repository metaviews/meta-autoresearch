# Hydrologic Whiplash and Design Failure

## Metadata

- title: Hydrologic Whiplash and Design Failure
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: stress-test oriented
- domain slice: climate volatility, water systems, infrastructure standards

## Research question

How might climate volatility undermine water and infrastructure planning by producing both flood-oriented and drought-oriented stress in the same systems faster than design standards can adapt?

## Why this scenario exists

Water planning is one of the clearest domains where historical baselines still quietly govern decision-making. This scenario is worth keeping because it turns the abstract claim that "stationarity is broken" into a concrete operational problem: infrastructure and operating rules designed for one distribution face a different one.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - many water systems still rely on historical records, return periods, and design assumptions that lag changing climate conditions
  - flood control, storage, drought response, and ecological management are often managed as partially separate planning problems
  - infrastructure has inertia: standards and operating rules change slower than conditions do
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the most important failure may come from the same place seeing both wet and dry extremes, not only from becoming simply wetter or drier
  - the planning problem is distribution shift and volatility, not only mean change
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a preparedness and design scenario, not a hydrologic model for a named basin
  - the focus is on decision and infrastructure stress, not on detailed engineering calculations

## Scenario logic

A basin or region experiences an intense precipitation period that strains drainage, reservoirs, river management, transport, and water quality systems. Operators respond using rules optimized for historically familiar flood-risk tradeoffs. Soon after, the pattern turns toward heat and drying. Reservoir levels that looked reassuring are drawn down faster than expected by demand, evaporation, ecological constraints, and earlier damage to infrastructure.

The institutional problem is not just that both extremes occur. It is that the standards, communication, and operational logic were tuned for a more stable distribution of streamflow timing, soil moisture, and seasonal transitions. Assets designed for one-sided optimization start failing in sequence: culverts, retention systems, reservoir rule curves, intake quality management, hydropower scheduling, irrigation planning, emergency water restrictions, and habitat protection rules.

What makes the scenario important is that each step can look manageable in isolation. The real failure is revealed only when the wet and dry stresses are treated as separate planning worlds even though the system now moves between them quickly enough to make that separation misleading.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - IPCC assessment of increasing heavy precipitation and increases in agricultural and ecological drought in some regions
  - IPCC assessment that complex risks result from interacting hazards and that water-related hazards increase with warming
  - broader planning concern that design standards are lagging changing climate conditions
- evidence classes consulted:
  - IPCC Working Group I and II summaries
  - water management and infrastructure resilience literature
  - reporting on flood-drought sequence stress and changing basin management challenges
- missing evidence:
  - concrete basin case studies where rapid wet-dry shifts exposed rule-curve or design-standard failure
  - clearer documentation of which infrastructure classes fail first under sequence stress

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: high; the mechanism is strongly aligned with IPCC findings on extremes and water-related risk
- internal coherence: high; the scenario consistently tracks how volatility breaks planning assumptions
- relevance: high; water systems are one of the clearest domains for applied preparedness thinking
- preparedness value: high; it points toward standards review, scenario-based operations, and cross-hazard planning
- novelty: medium-high; flood and drought are familiar separately, but the design-failure framing adds useful specificity

## Curation notes

- why keep this scenario:
  - it turns non-stationarity into a tangible infrastructure problem
  - it is distinct from food-system and seasonal-lag scenarios while still belonging to the same volatility family
  - it can support future note-taking around standards, return periods, and reservoir operations
- what should be refined next:
  - identify one or two real basins or cities as grounded variants
  - add examples of which operating assumptions break first
  - compare public-sector and utility-sector preparedness implications
- what might cause this scenario to be discarded or merged:
  - if it remains too abstract without case material
  - if a narrower flood-drought sequence scenario becomes more operationally useful

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how quickly planning rules can adapt in practice
  - how much failure is due to climate volatility versus legacy underinvestment and fragmented governance
  - which sectors experience the earliest compounding effects: water quality, agriculture, energy, or transport
- where this could be misleading:
  - it may imply that all design failure comes from climate when land use and governance are also central
  - it may overgeneralize across very different hydrologic regions
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that current adaptive water management already handles rapid wet-dry swings robustly in most cases
  - evidence that the main preparedness gap is not standards but only asset age or maintenance backlog
