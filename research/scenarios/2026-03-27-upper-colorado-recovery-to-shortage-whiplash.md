# Upper Colorado Recovery-to-Shortage Whiplash

## Metadata

- title: Upper Colorado Recovery-to-Shortage Whiplash
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: regional variant
- domain slice: Southwest climate volatility, snowpack interpretation, reservoir governance, institutional sequencing failure

## Research question

How might the Upper Colorado River Basin experience a preparedness failure when strong snowpack or drought-improvement signals are interpreted as durable recovery, only for runoff conversion limits, heat, and governance constraints to restore shortage conditions within the same planning cycle?

## Why this scenario exists

This scenario is the second named regional variant of the revised whiplash family. Unlike the Feather River case, which emphasizes wet-to-fire transition, this variant emphasizes recovery-to-shortage transition: the failure of snowpack relief to translate into secure runoff, reservoir resilience, and operational confidence.

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - the Upper Colorado Basin is a high-stakes water system serving tens of millions of people through downstream dependence
  - snowpack remains a powerful public and institutional signal for water optimism
  - runoff efficiency, reservoir operations, and governance constraints mediate whether apparent hydrologic recovery becomes real security
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the core failure is not only drought persistence, but misplaced confidence during partial recovery
  - recovery signals can increase vulnerability if they obscure the basin's dependence on runoff conversion and reservoir thresholds
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a basin-scale stress-test scenario, not a historical reconstruction of a single year
  - the focus is on planning interpretation, reservoir/governance consequences, and operational fragility rather than on full basin hydrology

## Scenario logic

After severe drought conditions, a strong winter snowpack and visible drought improvement create a regional sense of reprieve. Water managers, media narratives, and political actors begin to speak in recovery terms. The system does gain real short-term relief, but the interpretation outruns the conversion.

As the season progresses, runoff underperforms what snowpack alone seemed to promise. Antecedent dry soils, post-April heat, and wider aridity reduce the amount of water that actually reaches streams and reservoirs. The optimistic planning frame begins to crack. Reservoir anxiety returns, hydropower and shortage concerns reappear, and interstate governance remains tight because the basin was never restored as fully as the relief narrative implied.

The preparedness failure lies in the sequence: snowpack recovery, interpretive overcorrection, then renewed shortage stress. Institutions that still think in terms of drought versus recovery, rather than in terms of fragile conversion under non-stationary conditions, are more likely to misread the basin.

## Grounding

- Drought.gov reported record-setting snowpack and major drought improvement across the Southwest in 2023.
- Drought.gov also reports that Colorado River water supply can come in far below what snowpack alone would suggest because antecedent soil moisture and post-April weather strongly affect runoff.
- Colorado River contingency materials emphasize that reservoir levels remain tied to hydropower risk and compact compliance, even after temporary relief.
- NOAA-linked drought reporting shows that heat and human-caused warming intensify aridity and snowpack loss, making recovery more fragile.

These sources support a disciplined scenario in which relief is real but incomplete, and where the main whiplash comes from the failure of apparent recovery to become durable operating security.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - rapid swing from severe drought framing to partial relief framing
  - runoff forecast error relative to snowpack expectations
  - persistent reservoir and compact stress even during improved conditions
- evidence classes consulted:
  - Drought.gov briefings and basin explainers
  - NOAA-linked drought reporting
  - broader climate-aridity framing from the repo's existing evidence base
- missing evidence:
  - tighter case chronology for one specific year or cluster of years
  - more basin-specific planning or operator language showing interpretive overconfidence
  - stronger direct evidence linking recovery narratives to specific preparedness failures

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: high; the hydrologic and institutional mechanism is well supported by the available open-access evidence
- internal coherence: high; the scenario is tightly bounded and mechanically clear
- relevance: high; it directly tests a non-fire form of whiplash within the same family
- preparedness value: high; it highlights how to plan for fragile recovery rather than binary drought/recovery categories
- novelty: high; it reframes snowpack optimism as a potential source of planning error under non-stationarity
- status-quo challenge: high; it challenges the assumption that a good snow year means basin security is returning
- imaginative power: medium-high; it expands the family into governance and runoff-conversion failure without losing discipline

## Curation notes

- current curation gate:
  - keep
- why keep this scenario:
  - it gives the whiplash family a second named mechanism distinct from Feather River
  - it is strongly legible as a planning problem rather than only a descriptive climate story
  - it creates a useful comparison between fire-prone whiplash and reservoir-governance whiplash
- what should be refined next:
  - identify a specific seasonal window or operating year to anchor the basin story
  - add direct operator or policy language around snowpack expectations and runoff disappointment
  - compare Upper Basin interpretation with downstream reservoir politics
- what might cause this scenario to be revised or merged:
  - if the mechanism works better as a subtype of hydrologic whiplash rather than the broader seasonal family
  - if later evidence shows the apparent-recovery component was weaker than the runoff-conversion component alone

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how much of the whiplash comes from narrative overconfidence versus purely structural basin fragility
  - which planning institutions are most sequence-sensitive: forecast interpretation, reservoir operations, or compact governance
  - how to bound the scenario without flattening major Upper Basin differences
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply that snowpack improvement is illusory rather than partially real but insufficient
  - it could overstate the uniformity of basin response across subregions and institutions
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that planning systems already account well for snowpack-to-runoff conversion limits
  - evidence that the main failure is not sequence interpretation but only long-run structural scarcity
