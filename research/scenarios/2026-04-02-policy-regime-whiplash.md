# Policy Regime Whiplash

## Metadata

- title: Policy Regime Whiplash
- date: 2026-04-02
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- scenario type: non-climate sequence failure variant
- domain slice: political economy, policy volatility, institutional transition, regulatory whiplash

## Research question

How might rapid transitions between policy regimes (expansion-to-contraction, deregulation-to-reregulation, openness-to-restriction) create preparedness failures not because either regime is inherently flawed, but because institutions and actors optimized for one regime face whiplash when conditions shift faster than adaptation is possible?

## Why this scenario exists

The whiplash branch has strong climate sequence structure (wet-to-fire, recovery-to-shortage). This variant tests whether the sequence failure mechanism travels to non-climate domains. Policy whiplash shares structural features with climate whiplash (transition is the risk, not the endpoints; planning assumptions lag reality; separate policy "seasons" treated as independent) but differs in key ways (human agency, faster timescales, explicit political drivers).

## Framing and assumptions

- baseline assumptions:
  - policy regimes shift in response to crises, elections, and changing conditions
  - businesses, institutions, and individuals optimize for current regime expectations
  - adaptation to new regimes takes time (legal compliance, capital reallocation, behavioral change)
- assumptions that depart from default narratives:
  - the main failure is not regime A vs. regime B but the transition velocity between them
  - actors who correctly anticipate the direction of change can still fail if transition is faster than adaptation
  - treating policy "seasons" as independent (expansion normalcy, crisis exceptionalism) misses sequence risk
- boundaries of the scenario:
  - this is a sequence failure scenario, not a critique of specific policy directions
  - the focus is on transition dynamics, not regime evaluation

## Scenario logic

A policy domain experiences a period of one regime (e.g., expansion, deregulation, openness) long enough that actors optimize for its continuation. Capital is allocated, compliance systems are built, business models are designed around regime expectations. Then conditions shift (crisis, election, scandal, external shock) and policy rapidly transitions to the opposite regime (contraction, reregulation, restriction).

The preparedness failure is not that the new regime is wrong or the old regime was wrong. The failure is that the transition creates whiplash: compliance systems built for one regime malfunction under the other, capital allocated for one regime becomes stranded, expertise optimized for one regime becomes liability.

The institutional problem is compounded because policy domains are often managed as separate "seasons" (expansion planning, crisis response) with different teams, rules, and assumptions. The sequence—how fast one becomes the other, what carries over, what breaks—is not planned for.

## Grounding

- COVID policy transitions: rapid lockdown-to-reopening created supply chain and labor market whiplash
- Monetary policy: rapid easing-to-tightening cycles create financial market and business planning stress
- Trade policy: openness-to-restriction transitions (tariffs, export controls) disrupt supply chains
- Immigration policy: restriction-to-openness transitions create labor planning uncertainty
- Crypto/tech regulation: innovation permission-to-crackdown cycles create capital allocation stress

These cases support a policy whiplash scenario in which transition velocity, not regime direction, creates preparedness failure.

## Signals and evidence classes

- signals already visible:
  - increasing policy volatility across multiple domains (trade, tech, health, finance)
  - business surveys citing policy uncertainty as investment barrier
  - compliance costs rising with regulatory turnover
- evidence classes consulted:
  - policy uncertainty indices
  - regulatory turnover case studies
  - business adaptation and compliance literature
  - political economy of policy volatility
- missing evidence:
  - tighter quantification of adaptation timelines across policy domains
  - clearer case material on sequence failure vs. regime failure attribution
  - stronger comparison between fast-transition and gradual-transition outcomes

## Provisional evaluation

- plausibility: high; policy volatility and adaptation lag are well documented
- internal coherence: high; the sequence failure mechanism parallels climate whiplash clearly
- relevance: high; this tests whether whiplash structure generalizes beyond climate
- preparedness value: high; it points toward transition planning, adaptive compliance, scenario resilience
- novelty: high; it reframes policy risk as sequence failure, not regime evaluation
- status-quo challenge: high; it challenges partisan framing (good regime vs. bad regime) in favor of transition dynamics
- imaginative power: medium-high; it expands the branch into political economy sequence failure

## Curation notes

- current curation gate:
  - keep (tests non-climate portability of sequence failure structure)

  - it tests whether whiplash structure generalizes beyond climate
  - it adds a second non-climate domain to the whiplash branch (with wealth-concentration)
  - it enables comparison between climate and policy sequence mechanisms
- what should be refined next:
  - add specific regional/sector grounding (e.g., COVID reopening, monetary tightening cycle)
  - compare against wet-to-fire and recovery-to-shortage variants to identify structure similarities
  - clarify what counts as "transition too fast" vs. normal policy change
- what might cause this scenario to be revised or merged:
  - if policy whiplash proves fundamentally different from climate whiplash (agency changes structure)
  - if the scenario becomes too broad and loses the sequence failure mechanism

## Uncertainties and failure modes

- key uncertainties:
  - how fast is "too fast" for different domains and actors
  - whether some actors benefit from whiplash (arbitrage, political advantage)
  - how much adaptation is possible through scenario planning vs. inherent limits
- where this could be misleading:
  - it could imply all policy change creates whiplash (some transitions are gradual enough)
  - it could understate regime direction effects (some regimes are genuinely worse regardless of transition)
- what would challenge the scenario most:
  - evidence that adaptation timelines are short enough that whiplash is rare
  - evidence that sequence failures are actually regime failures misattributed
