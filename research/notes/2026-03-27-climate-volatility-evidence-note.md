## Purpose

Gather a first set of source-backed claims that can support early climate-volatility scenarios without overstating what the evidence shows.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Climate extremes are already changing in ways that matter for preparedness

The IPCC Working Group I summary states that human-induced climate change is already affecting many weather and climate extremes in every region. It says hot extremes have become more frequent and more intense across most land regions since the 1950s, that heavy precipitation events have increased over most land areas with sufficient observations, and that human influence has likely increased the chance of compound extreme events since the 1950s, including concurrent heatwaves and droughts on the global scale.

Why this matters here:

- the repo does not need to assume a speculative future to justify volatility-oriented scenario work
- compound events are not a fringe edge case; they are already part of the assessed climate signal

### 2. Risk increasingly comes from interacting hazards and interacting systems

The IPCC Working Group II summary says complex risks result from multiple climate hazards occurring concurrently and from multiple risks interacting, compounding overall risk and transmitting through interconnected systems and across regions. It also says observed increases in the frequency and intensity of climate and weather extremes have already produced widespread impacts on ecosystems, settlements, infrastructure, food security, water security, and health.

Why this matters here:

- it supports scenarios that focus on sequencing, overlap, and institutional lag rather than on isolated single-event stories
- it suggests preparedness failure may come as much from interaction effects as from absolute magnitude

### 3. Agricultural and food-system risk is not only local

Gaupp et al. (2020) argue that the risk of unusually low global agricultural production is exacerbated when more than one major breadbasket is exposed at the same time. Their abstract reports increasing risk of simultaneous failure of wheat, maize, and soybean crops across the breadbaskets analyzed, with implications for contingency planning and strategic reserves.

Why this matters here:

- it supports scenarios centered on correlated shocks rather than region-by-region independence
- it points toward food-system preparedness as a legitimate early scenario domain for this project

### 4. Co-occurring hot and dry extremes appear especially important for crops

Heino et al. (2023) find that co-occurring extremely hot and dry events have globally consistent negative effects on maize, rice, soybean, and wheat yields. Their study also reports that the probability of co-occurring hot and dry events during the growing season increased across all inspected crop types over the study period, with wheat showing the largest increase.

Why this matters here:

- it strengthens the case for treating compound heat-drought stress as a first-class scenario mechanism
- it suggests that climate-volatility scenarios should look at concurrent conditions, not only average seasonal changes

## Working implications for meta-autoresearch

- early scenarios should be designed around compound and cascading risk, not only single hazards
- the method should track where a scenario depends on concurrency, correlation, or rapid sequencing
- preparedness-oriented scenarios may be more useful than single-number forecasts in this phase
- food systems, water systems, and infrastructure systems are strong candidates for early scenario families

## Evidence-backed constraints

- these sources support stronger claims about changing extremes and compound risk than about any one local outcome
- they support scenario exploration and preparedness framing more than precise regional prediction
- they do not remove the need for local case evidence before making detailed operational claims

## Open questions

- which domains are most useful for the next scenario set: water, food, public health, or urban infrastructure?
- how should the repo represent correlation risk versus sequence risk?
- what qualitative measurement best distinguishes a useful preparedness scenario from a merely dramatic one?
