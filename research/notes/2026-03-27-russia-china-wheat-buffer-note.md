# Russia-China Wheat Buffer Note

## Purpose

Ground a Russia-China comparison variant for the wheat branch in which Russia remains the export and transmission node while China matters primarily as a production, stock, and demand-balancing buffer.

## Why this case

This is the best next contrast to the Russia-Europe trade variant because it tests a different system function. Europe matters as a substitute export buffer. China matters as a domestic balancing system whose production stress, stock management, and import behavior can tighten the global market without acting as a major exporter.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Russia and China play very different wheat-system roles

IndexMundi's 2024 balance-sheet pages show Russia at roughly `46,000` thousand metric tons of wheat exports, while China shows only about `1,000` thousand metric tons of wheat exports.

Why this matters:

- Russia remains the transmission node in this variant
- China should not be modeled as a parallel export buffer in the same way as Europe

### 2. China still matters enormously on the production side

The same balance-sheet material shows China at roughly `140,099` thousand metric tons of wheat production in 2024.

Why this matters:

- China can matter systemically even without major export leverage
- if a major domestic buffer weakens, global fallback assumptions become thinner

### 3. China also matters through stocks and internal balancing

IndexMundi reports roughly `133,102` thousand metric tons of Chinese wheat ending stocks in 2024. USDA FAS grain outlook reporting also treats wheat largely as part of China's domestic balancing system rather than as a major export channel.

Why this matters:

- the scenario mechanism is about stock use, retention, and import behavior
- the key question is whether China absorbs stress internally or transmits it into world demand

### 4. This variant is about production-buffer stress, not trade-buffer stress

Unlike Europe, China is not the cleanest substitute exporter for Russian wheat. Its importance lies elsewhere: if Chinese wheat production underperforms or if state stock and feed-balancing choices tighten, a large domestic system may pull harder on world markets instead of cushioning them.

Why this matters:

- it gives the branch a second mechanism family inside wheat itself
- it sharpens the difference between `trade substitution failure` and `buffer absorption failure`

## What this note supports

- creating a Russia-China production-buffer variant to compare against the Russia-Europe trade-buffer variant
- treating Chinese stocks and import decisions as a system-level risk factor under correlated wheat stress
- keeping export role and stock role analytically separate inside the breadbasket branch

## What this note does not support

- the claim that China is irrelevant to global wheat markets
- the claim that all Chinese stocks are fully liquid or instantly deployable
- the claim that Russia and China play interchangeable roles in wheat transmission

## Caution

Do not overread annual balance-sheet values. Stocks can vary in quality, location, and policy accessibility. The safer framing is that China matters as a domestic balancing and import-demand node, not as a mirror image of Russia's export role.

## Open questions

- what evidence best captures when Chinese stock use or import behavior starts affecting global procurement stress?
- how should the branch distinguish domestic stabilization from external market amplification?
- under what conditions would this variant be more decision-useful than the Russia-Europe trade-buffer variant?
