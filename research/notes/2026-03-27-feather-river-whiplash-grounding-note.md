## Purpose

Ground the revised `compound seasonal whiplash` family in a named California planning geography where wet-period relief, storage management, and later hot-dry/fire stress can plausibly interact.

## Why this case

The Northern Sierra and Feather River Canyon are useful because they sit at the intersection of water storage, snowpack, flood-space management, vegetation growth, transmission infrastructure, and extreme wildfire exposure. That makes the region a good test case for sequence blindness rather than only single-hazard thinking.

## Core takeaways

### 1. California planning already recognizes swing risk between extremes

California Water Watch explicitly frames the state as needing to manage swings between very dry and very wet conditions. That does not prove one specific local sequence, but it supports the larger premise that planning in California is increasingly shaped by volatility rather than by a stable seasonal baseline.

Why this matters:

- the whiplash family is not inventing volatility out of thin air
- the regional variant can be grounded in an already recognized planning problem rather than only in abstract method language

### 2. Northern California storage conditions can shift into apparent relief quickly

California Data Exchange Center reservoir summaries show that storage in the Sacramento River hydrologic region can move well above historical average in wet periods. This supports the scenario logic that a wet phase can create a sense of partial relief and encourage a planning reset toward abundance, even when underlying vulnerability remains.

Why this matters:

- a regional variant needs a plausible "relief" phase, not only a later crisis phase
- storage recovery can change public and institutional interpretation even if it does not resolve deeper risk

### 3. The same planning geography is also exposed to severe hot-dry/fire outcomes

CAL FIRE's 2021 incident records show that the Dixie Fire began in the Feather River Canyon and burned 963,309 acres. This demonstrates that the region is not only a water-management landscape but also a fire-prone one, making it suitable for a sequence-oriented scenario in which planning categories collide.

Why this matters:

- the region can plausibly move from water-relief framing to fire-emergency framing
- the scenario family becomes more concrete when tied to a geography already associated with both water and fire stress

## What this note supports

- creating a first named regional variant of the wet-to-hot-dry transition scenario family
- treating Northern Sierra planning as a sequence problem rather than only a flood problem or a fire problem
- further investigation into how reservoir, fuels, transmission, and emergency-planning systems interact in the same geography

## What this note does not support

- the claim that one specific wet year directly caused the Dixie Fire
- the claim that wet conditions mechanically produce later catastrophic fire
- the claim that the full causal sequence is already established without deeper case evidence

## Caution

The current evidence supports a plausible regional sequence and supports the broader volatility frame, but not a neat one-step causal story. The safer formulation is that wet periods can refill systems, increase biomass or fuel, and shift planning assumptions, while later hot-dry conditions can expose those assumptions as brittle.

## Open questions

- which year or short time window best captures a clear Feather River wet-to-hot-dry transition story?
- what role do transmission infrastructure, watershed management, and emergency planning play in the sequence?
- what evidence best distinguishes climate-volatility effects from broader governance and maintenance failures?
