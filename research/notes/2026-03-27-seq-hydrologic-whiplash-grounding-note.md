## Purpose

Ground the hydrologic branch in a named metro-scale system: South East Queensland, especially the Brisbane / Wivenhoe / Somerset / SEQ Water Grid context spanning the Millennium Drought and the 2010-11 flood sequence.

## Why this case

South East Queensland is a strong first hydrologic case because it combines long drought stress, major portfolio redesign, explicit drought triggers, a dual-purpose flood-mitigation / water-supply reservoir, and an abrupt shift into extreme flood conditions. It is one of the clearest places in the current evidence base where hydrologic volatility becomes an infrastructure and operating-rule problem rather than only a weather story.

## Core takeaways

### 1. The region was explicitly redesigned around water-security stress before the flood sequence

The 2007 South East Queensland water supply strategy review argued that a diverse portfolio of supply, demand, and drought-response options could secure supply well into the future without relying on one new dam. This supports the idea that the region had already moved into a planning mode shaped by hydrologic fragility and infrastructure redesign.

Why this matters:

- the case is not only about one flood event
- it shows that drought already altered the infrastructure and governance posture of the region

### 2. South East Queensland publicly frames itself as a climate-of-extremes system

Seqwater's water security page states that South East Queensland can be subject to a climate of extremes and documents trigger-based drought response tied to SEQ Water Grid storage levels, including desalination and recycled-water assets held as resilience options.

Why this matters:

- the hydrologic branch does not need to invent the volatility frame from outside the system
- the case naturally connects hydrologic variability to infrastructure portfolio design and operating thresholds

### 3. The water system is already a network built to manage uneven rainfall and regional imbalance

Seqwater describes the SEQ Water Grid as a network that moves treated drinking water around the region when patchy rainfall leaves some areas with full dams and other parts with lower dam levels.

Why this matters:

- the case supports a systems framing rather than a single-dam framing
- variability and mismatch across subregions are already central operating concerns

### 4. The flood side of the sequence was severe enough to expose dual-purpose rule tensions

The Bureau of Meteorology reports that Queensland had its wettest year on record in 2010 and describes one of the most significant floods in the state's history at the end of December, followed by extreme flooding in Brisbane and nearby areas in January 2011. Seqwater documents that Wivenhoe Dam has a dual purpose: water supply and flood mitigation, with flood operations governed by a dedicated manual.

Why this matters:

- the case directly links hydrologic extremes to live operating-rule tradeoffs
- the branch can be grounded in how the same system is asked to solve both storage security and flood-space management

## What this note supports

- creating a first named hydrologic variant rooted in South East Queensland
- treating the hydrologic branch as a portfolio, grid, and operating-rules problem rather than only a basin-hydrology problem
- comparing drought-security design with flood-mitigation stress inside one system

## What this note does not support

- the claim that Wivenhoe operations alone explain the 2011 flood damage
- the claim that climate change attribution is fully established by these sources alone
- the claim that all hydrologic systems share the same institutional structure as SEQ

## Caution

The strongest formulation is that South East Queensland is a documented case where drought-oriented security design, networked water infrastructure, and dual-purpose flood operations interact under extreme variability. It should not be simplified into a single blame story or a one-event morality play.

## Open questions

- which exact planning decisions changed most because of the Millennium Drought?
- how did flood-mitigation operations and water-supply logic interact in the most critical moments?
- what is the best comparison case for later: another urban grid system or a more agricultural basin?
