# India NBFC Transmission Note

## Purpose

Ground the transmission side of the wealth concentration branch in a more concrete downstream case: India's post-IL&FS non-bank funding squeeze, especially where tightening wholesale funding conditions hit smaller NBFCs and MFIs that provide last-mile credit.

## Why this case

This is a strong next grounding case because it shows how fragility can move through concentrated non-bank funding channels into real-economy credit access for smaller firms and low-income borrowers. It is not only a story about investor balance sheets; it is a story about withdrawal of intermediation and downstream resilience loss.

## Core takeaways

### 1. NBFCs had become systemically important credit conduits

IMF materials state that NBFCs were responsible for about half of lending growth during `2015-17`. RBI materials state that NBFCs had become the largest net borrowers of funds from the financial system and that more than half of their funding came from scheduled commercial banks.

Why this matters:

- the case is not a niche financial side story
- it shows a channel through which concentrated intermediation structures can matter for the broader economy

### 2. The funding withdrawal was real and measurable after IL&FS

The IMF reports that mutual funds, a key funding source, reduced NBFC commercial-paper exposure by almost `65%` since September `2018`. It also reports that NBFC lending growth to the nonfinancial sector fell to less than `5%` year over year in Q3 CY2019 from around `20%` in 2017/18.

Why this matters:

- the branch can now point to a visible intermediation-withdrawal mechanism rather than only a hypothetical one
- this strengthens the transmission interpretation considerably

### 3. Smaller last-mile lenders were hit harder

RBI states that after IL&FS and DHFL, market funding conditions turned difficult for NBFCs and that smaller NBFCs and Microfinance Institutions, which were contributing significantly to last-mile credit delivery, were squeezed further.

Why this matters:

- the downstream consequence is not uniform
- the case supports a branch logic in which fragility propagates unevenly through weaker transmission nodes into weaker borrowers

### 4. The real-economy stakes are large

RBI reports that microfinance serves `6 crore` borrowers and acts as a safety net for those at the bottom of the pyramid. It also states elsewhere that MSMEs employ around `11 crore` people, account for roughly `30%` of GDP, and `48%` of merchandise exports.

Why this matters:

- this gives the branch a concrete downstream resilience question
- a non-bank funding squeeze can become a livelihoods and working-capital problem, not just a funding-market event

## What this note supports

- a more concrete transmission-focused scenario inside wealth concentration
- a better comparison with the US asset-regime mismatch case
- a branch framing where concentrated funding and withdrawal of intermediation alter resilience far downstream from elite asset ownership

## What this note does not support

- the claim that IL&FS alone explains all downstream stress
- the claim that all NBFCs or all borrowers were hit equally
- the claim that Indian NBFCs are institutionally identical to US private credit

## Caution

The strongest framing is that the case shows how concentrated and opaque funding channels can squeeze downstream credit access when funding is withdrawn. Avoid overstating borrower outcomes beyond the evidence on credit and resilience pressure.
