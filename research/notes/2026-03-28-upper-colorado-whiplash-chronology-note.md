## Purpose

Tighten the Upper Colorado recovery-to-shortage whiplash case with a clearer relief-to-constraint chronology and more operator-facing evidence, without collapsing the branch into the hydrologic design-and-rule branch.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Relief language was explicit and strong in 2023

The April 2023 Southwest Drought Briefing reported record-setting snowpack, major short-term drought improvement across the region, and an April-July Lake Powell inflow forecast of `11.3 million acre-feet`, or `177%` of average.

Why this matters:

- the whiplash branch needs a credible relief phase, not only an analyst's reconstruction of one
- this gives the scenario direct evidence that optimism and recovery framing were institutionally legible

### 2. The relief was real, but official reporting still treated it as incomplete

Drought.gov's June 2023 western snow-drought summary states that the year brought major drought relief in the Colorado River Basin and above-normal runoff, but also says it would take much more than one wet year to refill Lakes Powell and Mead. The same source reports Powell at `37%` of capacity and Mead at `31%`.

Why this matters:

- the case is strongest when framed as `real but insufficient recovery`
- this supports a sequence in which relief signals and persistent structural stress coexist rather than cancel each other out

### 3. Snowpack and secure runoff are not the same planning signal

Drought.gov's Colorado River forecast-error note states that `2020` and `2021` had water supply much lower than snowpack values alone would suggest, and that "April 1 numbers do not tell the whole story" because antecedent soil moisture and April-June weather strongly affect water supply.

Why this matters:

- this gives the whiplash branch a clean operator-facing formulation of the core mechanism
- it sharpens the difference between visible recovery indicators and actual operating security

### 4. Basin-level forecast material shows how stark the divergence can be

The Colorado Basin River Forecast Center's April 2021 material shows Upper Basin snow-water equivalent values around `75-80%` in several sub-basins while also showing forecast runoff at some key points only `30-62%` of average, with soil moisture among the driest in the historical record.

Why this matters:

- it is strong evidence that planning signals can diverge even before reservoir politics fully enter the picture
- it reinforces that the whiplash branch is about interpretation and conversion, not only about storage rules

## What this note supports

- updating `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-upper-colorado-recovery-to-shortage-whiplash.md` with a tighter chronology
- framing the case as `real relief + persistent rule-bound stress`, not fake recovery
- keeping this branch distinct from the hydrologic design branch by centering interpretation and sequence rather than formal rule architecture alone

## What this note does not support

- the claim that 2023 relief was illusory
- the claim that all Upper Basin actors interpreted recovery the same way
- the claim that this branch is only a hydrologic design case under another name

## Caution

The cleanest formulation is that snowpack and drought-improvement signals can support real optimism while still failing to deliver durable operational security. That is different from claiming the relief was false, and different again from claiming the main issue was only long-run legal or reservoir design stress.
