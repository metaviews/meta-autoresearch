# Upper Colorado Whiplash Grounding Note

## Purpose

Ground a second named variant of the `compound seasonal whiplash` family in the Upper Colorado River Basin, using a different mechanism from the Feather River case: apparent snowpack or drought relief that fails to convert into durable reservoir and water-supply security.

## Why this case

The Upper Colorado complements Feather River well because it emphasizes hydrologic and institutional whiplash rather than wet-growth-to-fire whiplash. The main failure mode here is not fuel loading and wildfire exposure, but the brittle assumption that a strong snow year or drought improvement automatically translates into secure runoff, reservoir recovery, and operational stability.

## Core takeaways

### 1. The Southwest can move quickly from severe drought framing to apparent relief

The June 2023 Southwest Drought Briefing from Drought.gov states that the region saw record-setting snowpack over the previous winter and that drought improved dramatically across the Southwest from 97% of the region in drought in June 2022 to 9% in June 2023.

Why this matters:

- this gives the scenario a credible relief phase rather than only a scarcity phase
- it shows how quickly institutional and public interpretation can swing toward recovery

### 2. Snowpack does not reliably convert into water-supply security

Drought.gov's Colorado River water-supply forecast note states that in 2020 and 2021, Colorado River water supply was much lower than snowpack values alone would suggest. It emphasizes that April 1 snowpack does not tell the whole story because antecedent soil moisture and post-April weather strongly affect runoff and streamflow.

Why this matters:

- the whiplash mechanism is not simply climate variability but runoff-conversion failure
- relief signals can be real and still misleading if planners overread them

### 3. Reservoir and governance stress persist even when short-term conditions improve

Drought.gov's Colorado River drought contingency page states that the Colorado River provides water to almost 40 million people and that, since 2000, historically dry conditions have increased the risk of critically low elevations at Lakes Powell and Mead nearly four-fold. It also notes that critically low reservoir levels could affect hydropower generation and Colorado River Compact compliance.

Why this matters:

- the Upper Colorado case turns whiplash into a governance and infrastructure problem, not just a meteorological one
- short-term relief can coexist with deep structural exposure

### 4. Human-caused warming worsens the conversion problem

Drought.gov's summary of the NOAA Southwest drought report states that from January 2020 to August 2021 the Southwest had its lowest precipitation totals on record and third-highest daily average temperatures since 1895, and that human-caused warming accelerated snowpack losses and increased aridity.

Why this matters:

- it supports a whiplash frame in which heat and aridity can erode the practical value of apparent recovery
- it links the case to the broader non-stationarity problem rather than a single unusual year

## What this note supports

- creating a second named whiplash variant with a different failure mechanism from Feather River
- treating the Upper Colorado Basin as a case of recovery-to-shortage whiplash rather than only drought persistence
- comparing fire-prone sequence failure with runoff/governance sequence failure inside the same scenario family

## What this note does not support

- the claim that snowpack recovery is meaningless
- the claim that one good snow year directly causes later shortage
- the claim that the entire Southwest behaves as one uniform system

## Caution

The safer framing is not that apparent recovery is fake. It is that short-term recovery signals can fail to deliver durable operational security when runoff efficiency, post-snow weather, soil moisture deficits, reservoir dynamics, and governance constraints all matter.

## Open questions

- which specific Upper Basin operating geography should anchor the first scenario variant: Lake Powell, a headwaters zone, or the basin more broadly?
- what is the best year cluster for illustrating recovery-to-shortage whiplash without oversimplifying the basin?
- how should the scenario represent governance constraints alongside hydrologic ones?
