## Purpose

Ground the first non-climate proving-ground branch in a structural view of increasing wealth concentration: not only as a distributional outcome, but as a system of feedback loops, portfolio asymmetries, and institutionally mediated compounding.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Wealth concentration is already extreme at the global level

The World Inequality Report 2022 states that in 2021 the global top `10%` owned `76%` of household wealth, the middle `40%` owned `22%`, and the bottom `50%` owned just `2%`. The same source reports that billionaires' share of global wealth rose from about `1%` in 1995 to over `3%` by 2021.

Why this matters:

- the branch does not need a speculative starting point; concentration is already a dominant structural fact
- the method can start from an existing imbalance and ask what hidden dynamics keep reproducing it

### 2. The top captures gains disproportionately over time

WID reports that the top `1%` captured `38%` of all additional wealth accumulated since the mid-1990s, while the bottom `50%` captured just `2%`.

Why this matters:

- the issue is not only a static stock of wealth
- it is a compounding system in which gains flow upward at a far faster rate than they diffuse downward

### 3. Wealth concentration is linked to portfolio structure and institutional design

WID argues that rising wealth concentration is linked not only to income inequality, but also to unequal savings rates, unequal rates of return, privatization, financial deregulation, and lower progressive taxation. It also notes that very wealthy groups hold much more of their wealth in financial assets, while middle-class households are more housing-heavy.

Why this matters:

- the branch can treat concentration as an institutional and asset-structure problem, not as a moral snapshot only
- the mechanism is not simply that some people have more money, but that some portfolios and rule environments compound more effectively than others

### 4. National institutions are central to the dynamics

WID's discussion of global inequality argues that since around the turn of the 21st century, within-country inequality has become more important than between-country inequality, making national pre-distribution and redistribution of income and capital central to global inequality dynamics.

Why this matters:

- the branch likely needs to pay attention to policy architecture, tax design, asset regimes, and institutional lock-in
- this supports testing `design/rule conflict` and `correlation/transmission` mechanisms within the same domain

### 5. Hidden balance-sheet channels may transmit fragility unevenly

A BIS working paper shows that non-financial corporations can act as under-recognized financial intermediaries by borrowing cheaper foreign-currency credit and re-lending domestically through trade credit. It finds that when exchange-rate shocks hit, firms with greater carry-trade exposure cut investment and profits more, even though trade-credit networks remain stable.

Why this matters:

- concentration may matter not only because wealth is unequally held, but because concentrated balance-sheet positions can transmit fragility through opaque channels
- this suggests the branch may expose hidden transmission structures rather than only visible inequality ratios

### 6. Existing wealth-tax structures can reinforce concentration unintentionally

WID argues that existing wealth taxation is often effectively regressive because property taxes hit housing while top wealth holders increasingly hold financial assets.

Why this matters:

- the branch may reveal `design/rule conflict` directly if formal tax design no longer maps well onto the asset structures of concentrated wealth
- this makes wealth concentration a promising non-climate test of the method's third structure type

## Promising structural framings

- `portfolio-size feedback loop`
  - larger fortunes capture higher returns, save more, and hold more scalable assets, so concentration compounds structurally
- `public-to-private transfer plus asset-price amplification`
  - privatization, deregulation, and weakened progressive taxation move claims upward and then asset appreciation multiplies the shift
- `hidden intermediation / fragility channel`
  - concentration shapes credit, investment, and shock transmission through under-recognized balance-sheet networks

## Best first scenario direction

The strongest first scenario framing is:

- wealth concentration as a `self-reinforcing balance-sheet system`, not just a distributional snapshot

That framing is likely to support a stronger proving-ground pass than a simple inequality narrative because it can connect:

- asset structure
- rates of return
- institutional design
- hidden transmission channels
- downstream fragility

## What this note supports

- a first branch scenario focused on compounding balance-sheet lock-in
- testing whether wealth concentration maps most strongly to `correlation/transmission failure`, `design/rule conflict under volatility`, or a hybrid of both
- a non-climate proving-ground pass that still preserves the project's anti-prediction posture

## What this note does not support

- the claim that one universal mechanism explains all wealth concentration
- the claim that billionaire growth alone explains the whole problem
- the claim that any one policy reform would necessarily reverse the system cleanly

## Caution

The strongest framing is that wealth concentration is institutionally contingent and structurally compounding. Avoid overstating precision in global estimates or implying that one causal story applies everywhere.
