# Rice Climate-Yield Evidence Note

**Date:** 2026-04-02  
**Branch:** breadbasket  
**Type:** Grounding note  
**Status:** draft

---

## Purpose

This note grounds the Southeast Asia rice correlation shock scenario in climate-yield evidence, parallel to how Heino et al. (2023) grounds the wheat variants.

**Question:** What evidence supports correlated hot-dry stress affecting India, Thailand, and Vietnam rice production simultaneously?

---

## Evidence Base

### 1. Rice Climate Sensitivity

**Source:** FAO Rice Market Monitor (various issues)  
**Finding:** Rice yields are sensitive to both temperature and precipitation during critical growth stages (flowering, grain filling). Hot-dry conditions during these stages can reduce yields by 10-20% per event.

**Relevance:** Supports the mechanism that climate stress can affect rice production, but does not yet establish correlation across regions.

---

### 2. Monsoon Correlation Patterns

**Source:** Climate studies on South and Southeast Asian monsoon variability  
**Finding:** The South Asian and Southeast Asian monsoons are teleconnected through ENSO (El Niño-Southern Oscillation). El Niño years tend to produce weaker monsoons across both India and Southeast Asia simultaneously.

**Key findings:**
- El Niño events correlate with reduced rainfall in India and Southeast Asia
- 2015-2016 El Niño produced simultaneous stress across both regions
- Climate change may increase El Niño frequency and intensity

**Relevance:** Supports the correlation mechanism—climate stress can affect India and Southeast Asia in the same season.

---

### 3. Historical Rice Production Shortfalls

**Source:** USDA FAS rice production data, FAO statistics  
**Finding:** Documented cases of simultaneous production stress:
- 2015: India (El Niño drought) + Thailand (below-average monsoon)
- 2019: India (monsoon deficit) + Vietnam (Mekong Delta drought)
- 2023: India (monsoon variability, export restriction) + Thailand (heat stress)

**Relevance:** Provides historical precedents for correlated stress, though not all cases involved severe trade disruption.

---

### 4. Export Restriction Precedents

**Source:** FAO, news reports on rice trade policy  
**Finding:** India has imposed rice export restrictions multiple times:
- 2023: Non-basmati rice export ban (cited domestic price concerns)
- 2011: Export restrictions during global food price concerns
- Thailand and Vietnam have coordinated on rice trade policy (though not always restrictive)

**Relevance:** Supports the policy amplification mechanism—production stress can trigger export restrictions that amplify trade shocks.

---

### 5. Thin Trade Market Structure

**Source:** USDA FAS, FAO trade data  
**Finding:** Rice trade is 5-10% of production (vs. 20%+ for wheat):
- India: ~40% of global rice trade
- Thailand + Vietnam: ~35% of global rice trade
- Top 3 exporters control ~75% of trade

**Relevance:** Supports the acute correlation subtype—thin market means small production shortfalls create large trade shocks.

---

## Key Findings

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Source Type |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| Rice yields sensitive to hot-dry stress | High | FAO monitoring, climate studies |
| India and Southeast Asia monsoons are correlated | High | Climate teleconnection studies |
| Historical simultaneous stress events occurred | Moderate | Production statistics (2015, 2019, 2023) |
| Export restrictions amplify trade shocks | High | Documented policy cases (2023, 2011) |
| Rice trade is thin and concentrated | High | USDA, FAO trade data |

## Tensions and Gaps

### What the Evidence Supports
- Climate-yield mechanism for rice is well-established
- Monsoon correlation through ENSO is documented
- Export restriction precedents exist

### What Remains Uncertain
- **Specific correlation magnitude:** How often do India-Thailand-Vietnam stress events occur simultaneously?
- **Climate change impact:** Will correlation increase or decrease with warming?
- **Substitution effects:** Can importers shift to wheat or other grains quickly enough?

### Missing Evidence
- Peer-reviewed study on rice correlation parallel to Heino et al. (2023) for wheat
- Quantified probability of simultaneous India-Thailand-Vietnam stress
- Case study of actual trade disruption from correlated rice stress (vs. single-country events)

---

## Implications for Rice Scenario

### What This Grounding Supports
1. **Climate-yield mechanism:** Hot-dry stress affecting rice production is well-documented
2. **Correlation pathway:** ENSO teleconnection provides physical basis for simultaneous stress
3. **Policy amplification:** Export restriction precedents support the transmission mechanism
4. **Acute subtype:** Thin trade market and high concentration support "acute correlation" classification

### What Needs More Work
1. **Specific correlation probability:** Need quantified likelihood of simultaneous stress
2. **Climate change projection:** How will correlation change with warming?
3. **Importer resilience:** How much substitution is actually possible?
