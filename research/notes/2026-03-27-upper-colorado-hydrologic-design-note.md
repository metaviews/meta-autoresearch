# Upper Colorado Hydrologic Design Note

## Metadata

- title: Upper Colorado Hydrologic Design Note
- date: 2026-03-27
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- type: case note

## Purpose

Ground a second hydrologic branch case in the Upper Colorado River Basin, especially the Lake Powell / Lake Mead operating regime, to contrast with South East Queensland's networked urban water-grid case.

## Why this case

The Upper Colorado is the strongest contrast to South East Queensland because it is not primarily a metro drinking-water grid. It is a basin-scale storage, allocation, and rule system shaped by compact law, interstate obligations, reservoir thresholds, and shortage planning. This makes it ideal for testing whether hydrologic whiplash can also appear as rule conflict and compact stress under long-duration variability.

## Core takeaways

### 1. The basin was built around explicit allocation and storage assumptions

The 1922 Colorado River Compact states that its purposes include equitable apportionment, agricultural and industrial development, storage, and protection of life and property from floods. It apportions `7.5 million acre-feet` annually to both the Upper and Lower Basins, provides the Lower Basin an additional `1 million acre-feet`, and requires the Upper Division not to deplete Lee Ferry below `75 million acre-feet` over any `10` consecutive years.

Why this matters:

- the branch can be grounded in a rule system that is explicit rather than implicit
- hydrologic volatility here becomes a conflict among legal allocation, storage logic, and operating thresholds

### 2. The Colorado system is a high-consequence infrastructure and allocation regime

Reclamation states that the basin provides water to `35-40 million` people and that agriculture uses `70%` of Colorado River water.

Why this matters:

- this is not a marginal operating problem
- any hydrologic design or rule conflict here has major downstream preparedness implications

### 3. Hydrologic variability has already forced new operating layers and emergency measures

Reclamation's Drought Contingency Plan page states that the Upper and Lower Basin DCPs were signed in `2019`, that the Upper Basin DCP is meant to reduce the risk of critical elevations at Lake Powell and support Compact compliance, that the DROA uses releases from Flaming Gorge, Aspinall, and Navajo when Lake Powell is projected to approach elevation `3,525 feet`, and that `2022` brought the first Lower Basin shortage declaration.

Why this matters:

- the case already shows that the inherited design and allocation regime has needed active rule revision under stress
- hydrologic variability is not theoretical here; it is already altering operations

### 4. The basin is entering another formal redesign phase

Reclamation states that the `2007 Interim Guidelines`, the `2019 Drought Contingency Plans`, and related operating agreements expire at the end of `2026`, and that the post-2026 process will determine Lake Powell and Lake Mead operations for potentially decades.

Why this matters:

- the case is live, not historical only
- it supports the branch as a design-and-rule volatility problem rather than a one-time drought story

## What this note supports

- a second named hydrologic variant centered on compact law, reservoir thresholds, and shortage-rule redesign
- direct comparison between an urban water-grid case and a basin allocation/storage regime
- treating hydrologic volatility as institutional rule stress, not only physical hydrology

## What this note does not support

- the claim that the 1922 Compact alone explains the present crisis
- the claim that the Upper Colorado is simply a flood-drought mirror of South East Queensland
- the claim that one rule failure explains the full basin problem

## Caution

The strongest framing is that hydrologic variability exposed tensions in a system built around explicit allocation, storage, and delivery assumptions. This is not a clean blame story. It is a layered conflict among law, infrastructure, operating thresholds, and changing hydrologic reality.

## Open questions

- which operating thresholds are most useful for a bounded variant: Lake Powell elevation, Compact compliance, or shortage declarations?
- how should the branch distinguish long-duration aridification from shorter-term hydrologic whiplash?
- does this case represent a distinct hydrologic structure or a subtype of sequence failure?
