# US Asset-Regime Mismatch Note

## Metadata

- title: US Asset-Regime Mismatch Note
- date: 2026-03-28
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- type: case note

## Purpose

Ground the next wealth concentration pass in a bounded US case: a wealth-tax and asset-regime mismatch in which housing-heavy taxation and realization-based rules fit middle-class balance sheets better than the business and financial asset structures through which top wealth compounds.

## Why this case

This is a strong first bounded grounding case because it tests `design/rule conflict under volatility` outside climate in a concrete institutional setting. It avoids vague inequality language by asking whether the rule architecture still maps to the asset forms that dominate concentrated wealth.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Top wealth and middle wealth are not held in the same kinds of assets

The World Inequality Report states that in rich countries middle classes mainly own bank deposits and real estate, while the top decile owns much more business and financial wealth. It reports that financial assets can represent `90-95%` of wealth in the very top groups in countries such as France or the United States. The Federal Reserve's 2022 family finances survey also states that middle balance sheets are dominated by housing, while privately held business ownership is much more common in the top usual-income decile than in the bottom half.

Why this matters:

- the same tax architecture does not hit the same wealth forms equally
- the branch can now focus on a concrete mismatch between asset structure and rule structure

### 2. Existing wealth taxation is still heavily property-centered

WID argues that most countries already tax wealth mainly through property and inheritance taxes, and that property taxes account for `80-100%` of tax revenue on individual wealth. It also states that property taxes mainly hit real estate and land, while the wealthiest hold more financial assets, making the existing system increasingly disconnected from the current socio-economic reality.

Why this matters:

- this supports a direct `design/rule conflict` interpretation
- the branch can now ask whether the tax system is targeting the wrong balance-sheet forms

### 3. Realization and inherited-basis rules can defer taxation on appreciated assets

IRS materials state that capital gain is measured relative to asset basis and that inherited property generally takes basis equal to fair market value at the date of death. The IRS estate-tax page also states that estate tax is levied on transfer at death and filing applies only above a high threshold (`$13.99 million` for 2025 and `$15.0 million` for 2026).

Why this matters:

- the branch can point to a concrete rule path through which appreciated asset pools may compound for long periods before a transfer-tax event is triggered
- this makes the structural mismatch more specific than a generic complaint about low taxation

## What this note supports

- creating a first bounded wealth-concentration sub-branch around US asset-regime mismatch
- testing whether wealth concentration outside climate maps cleanly into `design/rule conflict under volatility`
- distinguishing property-heavy taxation from financial-asset-heavy wealth compounding

## What this note does not support

- the claim that property taxation is regressive in every jurisdiction or every form
- the claim that basis rules or estate-tax thresholds alone explain wealth concentration
- the claim that the US case automatically generalizes to all countries

## Caution

The strongest formulation is that current tax and asset rules often map imperfectly onto the structures through which concentrated wealth now compounds. This is more precise than saying top wealth is simply untaxed.
