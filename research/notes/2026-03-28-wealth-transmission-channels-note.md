# Wealth Transmission Channels Note

- title: Wealth Transmission Channels Note
- date: 2026-03-28
- author: OpenCode

- type: case note

## Purpose

Ground the wealth concentration branch in a more transmission-focused direction by looking at hidden intermediation channels, especially non-bank finance and private credit, where concentrated capital allocation can shape fragility and downstream resilience in ways that are hard to see from inequality snapshots alone.

## Why this case

This is the strongest current contrast to the US asset-regime mismatch branch. It does not begin with tax architecture. It begins with the balance-sheet and intermediation pathways through which concentrated capital positions can delay, redirect, or amplify stress across the real economy.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Non-bank intermediation is now large enough to matter structurally

The FSB reports that global NBFI assets reached `$217.9 trillion` in 2022, or `47.2%` of total global financial assets. Its narrower, vulnerability-focused measure of NBFI was `$63.1 trillion`, or `13.9%` of total global financial assets.

Why this matters:

- hidden transmission channels are not marginal edge cases
- the branch can now point to system-scale balance-sheet structures rather than only to abstract concentration logic

### 2. Private credit is a strong bounded proving-ground case

The IMF reports that private credit reached `$2.1 trillion` globally, with about three-quarters in the United States. It also notes that more than one-third of borrowers now have interest costs exceeding current earnings.

Why this matters:

- this provides a bounded high-concentration transmission case inside the broader wealth branch
- it creates a path from concentrated capital allocation to real-economy financing stress

### 3. Fragility can hide in leverage, valuation, and interconnected ownership channels

The IMF notes vulnerability channels including stale and subjective valuations, multiple layers of hidden leverage, and linkages among private credit funds, private equity firms, banks, insurers, and pension funds.

Why this matters:

- this is a direct transmission-oriented mechanism
- the branch can now ask how concentrated wealth affects stress propagation through under-recognized balance-sheet networks

### 4. The downstream effect may be withdrawal of intermediation, not immediate collapse

The IMF warns that in stress, insurers and pension funds may need to sell more liquid assets, spreading strain beyond private credit itself. The World Bank's de-risking material also shows that when financial institutions retreat from certain channels, smaller jurisdictions and firms can lose access rather than simply suffer mark-to-market losses.

Why this matters:

- the key consequence may be a resilience squeeze rather than a visible crash
- this gives the branch a cleaner `correlation/transmission failure` test than the tax-design case

### 5. Buffers can delay stress rather than remove it

The BIS notes that many non-financial corporates entered tightening with long-maturity and fixed-rate debt plus liquidity buffers, which delayed immediate debt-service pressure. It also notes that this shielding is temporary because significant debt will mature over the next three to six years.

Why this matters:

- this opens a temporal element inside the transmission story, but it is still mainly about how stress moves through financial structure
- the branch can distinguish delayed fragility from true resilience

## Best first transmission scenario direction

The strongest bounded transmission framing is:

- `private-credit stress triggers a downstream resilience squeeze`

This is a good proving-ground case because it connects:

- concentrated capital allocation
- opaque valuation and leverage structures
- intermediation withdrawal
- real-economy refinancing and working-capital stress

## What this note supports

- creating a transmission-focused contrasting branch inside wealth concentration
- testing whether wealth concentration is better explained through hidden system transmission than through asset-regime mismatch alone
- comparing a rule-centered case against a transmission-centered case within the same domain

## What this note does not support

- the claim that non-bank scale alone means imminent crisis
- the claim that private credit is already systemically dominant everywhere
- the claim that wealth concentration itself directly causes every downstream financing problem

## Caution

The strongest formulation is that concentrated wealth and opaque intermediation shape how fragility travels. Avoid overstating immediacy or claiming that size alone proves systemic failure.
