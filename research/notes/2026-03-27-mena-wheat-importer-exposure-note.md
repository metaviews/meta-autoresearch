## Purpose

Add a downstream preparedness layer to the breadbasket branch by distinguishing wheat-import exposure across selected MENA countries rather than treating the region as a single bucket.

## Why this note matters

The breadbasket branch increasingly shows that physical crop stress is only part of the problem. Downstream exposure depends on import dependence, reserve duration, procurement capacity, subsidy systems, foreign-exchange access, and existing household vulnerability. This note is meant to keep those layers explicit.

## Core takeaways

### 1. Egypt is a scale case where import dependence and procurement policy are central

FAO GIEWS reports wheat import requirements for Egypt at roughly `13 million tonnes` and states that imports cover more than half of annual consumption. The same source notes government intervention on procurement prices.

Why this matters:

- Egypt is a key case where procurement settings and subsidy capacity are as important as upstream harvest shocks
- preparedness needs to track tenders, financing, and milling throughput, not only world wheat prices

### 2. Jordan shows that import dependence and short-run resilience are not the same thing

FAO GIEWS reports that Jordan's imports cover nearly `70 percent` of annual domestic consumption, but also states that Jordan maintains strategic wheat stocks equivalent to about `ten months` of local consumption.

Why this matters:

- reserve cover can materially change short-run exposure
- the branch should not equate import dependence with immediate disruption

### 3. Lebanon shows how import exposure becomes more dangerous in already fragile systems

FAO GIEWS reports wheat import requirements in the `2025/26` marketing year at about `680,000 tonnes`, above average, while also reporting large populations facing crisis-level food insecurity.

Why this matters:

- downstream impact depends on household access and system fragility, not only on wheat-market tightness
- the branch should pair procurement exposure with distributional vulnerability

### 4. Yemen is the strongest procurement-sensitivity case in this set

FAO GIEWS reports that Yemen's wheat imports largely depend on the capacity of commercial importers and that limited foreign-currency reserves remain a challenge for buying cereals on international markets. The same source reports very high levels of acute food insecurity.

Why this matters:

- importer balance sheets, FX access, and logistics are central chokepoints
- this is the clearest case where market tightening can interact with severe existing vulnerability

### 5. Tunisia is a useful middle case

FAO GIEWS reports that Tunisia's local wheat production covers about `60 percent` of domestic annual needs and that the country still relies on international markets for adequate supply.

Why this matters:

- Tunisia helps show that MENA exposure is not binary
- it gives the branch a middle case between acute crisis settings and stronger state-buffer settings

## Preparedness implications

- track reserve duration, not just import dependence
- distinguish procurement sensitivity from household vulnerability
- monitor FX access, subsidies, and commercial importer capacity alongside price exposure
- avoid treating MENA as a single downstream risk category

## What this note supports

- stronger downstream grounding for wheat-branch scenarios
- importer-facing preparedness analysis for Russia-Europe and Russia-China variants
- a more explicit link between breadbasket stress and unequal access outcomes

## What this note does not support

- the claim that all MENA countries are equally exposed
- the claim that wheat shocks alone explain downstream crises
- the claim that import dependence automatically implies immediate scarcity

## Caution

Country briefs are snapshot-style assessments. Use them to distinguish exposure types, not to make deterministic forecasts. Bread outcomes depend on multiple chokepoints: ports, finance, tenders, subsidies, milling, and household purchasing power.
