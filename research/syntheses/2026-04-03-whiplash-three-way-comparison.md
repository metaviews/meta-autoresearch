# Whiplash Three-Way: Climate vs. Policy vs. Hybrid Sequence

**Date:** 2026-04-03  
**Type:** Comparison synthesis  
**Branch:** whiplash  
**Status:** draft

---

## Purpose

This synthesis extends the climate vs. policy sequence comparison to include the hybrid climate-policy whiplash variant, testing whether the sequence failure structure handles mixed agentic/non-agentic cases.

**Questions this answers:**
1. Does the hybrid sequence (climate policy) behave like climate, like policy, or like something different?
2. Is hybrid sequence more dangerous than pure sequences or just different?
3. What does this teach about the structure type's boundaries?

---

## Variants Compared

| Aspect | Climate (Feather River) | Policy (Regime Whiplash) | Hybrid (Climate Policy) |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **Sequence type** | Wet-to-fire transition | Expansion-to-contraction regime | Mitigation-to-adaptation-to-emergency |
| **Drivers** | Non-agentic (climate volatility) | Agentic (political change, crises) | Mixed (non-agentic triggers agentic response) |
| **Timescale** | Months to years | Months to years (election cycles) | Years to decades (policy + climate) |
| **Predictability** | Increasingly unpredictable | Partially predictable (elections) | Both unpredictable in different ways |
| **Adaptation lag** | Infrastructure rules lag conditions | Capital/compliance lag policy change | Both infrastructure AND policy lag |
| **Reversibility** | Slow (infrastructure changes slowly) | Fast (policy can reverse quickly) | Asymmetric (policy fast, infrastructure slow) |
| **Beneficiaries** | None documented | Some actors benefit (arbitrage, political) | Both types may benefit |
| **Compound risk** | Single domain (climate) | Single domain (political) | Dual domain (climate + political) |

## Shared Mechanisms (Structure Holds)

All three variants exhibit the core sequence failure mechanism:

1. **Transition is the risk** — Not the endpoints but the shift between regimes
2. **Optimization for one regime** — Actors optimize for current conditions, creating vulnerability
3. **Separate planning categories** — Wet/fire, expansion/contraction, mitigation/adaptation planned independently
4. **Adaptation lag** — Rules, capital, expertise optimized for one regime malfunction under the other
5. **Institutional inertia** — Systems change slower than conditions

**Judgment:** The sequence failure structure **holds across all three variants**, including the hybrid.

---

## Hybrid-Specific Findings

The climate-policy whiplash variant exhibits **unique characteristics** not present in pure sequences:

### 1. Asymmetric Reversibility
- Policy can reverse quickly (election, executive order)
- Infrastructure cannot reverse quickly (dams, grids take decades)
- Creates compound stress: policy shifts but infrastructure remains misaligned

### 2. Dual Unpredictability
- Climate volatility is becoming less predictable (non-stationarity)
- Political transitions are partially predictable (elections) but crisis timing is not
- Combined: two independent unpredictability sources compound

### 3. Compound Beneficiaries
- Climate sequence: no documented beneficiaries
- Policy sequence: arbitrage, political advantage beneficiaries
- Hybrid: both types may benefit simultaneously (climate arbitrage + political advantage)

### 4. Dual Adaptation Requirements
- Must adapt infrastructure (slow, expensive)
- Must adapt policy compliance (faster, but still significant)
- Both required simultaneously creates compounding adaptation burden

**Judgment:** Hybrid sequence is **not just different—it is more dangerous** than pure sequences due to asymmetric reversibility, dual unpredictability, and compounding adaptation burden.

## Structure Refinement: Sequence Subtypes

Based on this three-way comparison, the sequence failure structure has **three subtypes**:

### Subtype A: Non-Agentic Sequence (Climate)
- Drivers are physical (climate, hydrology)
- Adaptation lag is infrastructural
- Predictability is declining
- No beneficiaries
- Example: Feather River wet-to-fire

### Subtype B: Agentic Sequence (Policy)
- Drivers are political (elections, crises, ideology)
- Adaptation lag is behavioral and capital-based
- Predictability is partial
- Some beneficiaries
- Example: COVID lockdown-to-reopening, monetary tightening

### Subtype C: Hybrid Sequence (Climate Policy)
- Drivers are mixed (physical triggers political response)
- Adaptation lag is both infrastructural and behavioral
- Predictability is dual (both sources unpredictable)
- Multiple beneficiary types
- **More dangerous than pure sequences**
- Example: Mitigation-to-adaptation transition during climate volatility

## Method Lesson

**What this teaches the method:**

1. **Hybrid sequences are more dangerous than pure sequences** — The combination of agentic and non-agentic drivers creates compounding risks that neither alone produces.

2. **Asymmetric reversibility is a key parameter** — When one component of a sequence can reverse quickly (policy) but another cannot (infrastructure), the mismatch creates unique failure modes.

3. **Dual unpredictability compounds** — Two independent sources of unpredictability (climate + political) create more than additive risk.

4. **Structure subtypes matter for preparedness** — Not all sequence failures are equally dangerous. Hybrid sequences require different preparedness strategies than pure sequences.
