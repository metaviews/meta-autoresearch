## Purpose

Determine whether the wealth concentration proving-ground branch is beginning to resolve toward a dominant structure type by directly comparing its two strongest grounded sub-branches:

- `research/scenarios/2026-03-28-us-asset-regime-and-wealth-lock-in.md`
- `research/scenarios/2026-03-28-private-credit-resilience-squeeze.md`

## The two strongest sub-branches

### 1. US Asset-Regime and Wealth Lock-In

Primary structural read:

- `design/rule conflict under volatility`

Core logic:

- tax and transfer rules map more directly onto housing-heavy middle wealth than onto the financial and business asset structures through which concentrated wealth compounds

### 2. Private-Credit Resilience Squeeze

Primary structural read:

- `correlation/transmission failure`

Core logic:

- concentrated capital allocation, opaque valuation, leverage, and intermediation withdrawal shape how fragility propagates through the broader economy

## Main comparison

### Which is more bounded?

`US Asset-Regime and Wealth Lock-In`

Why:

- it names a clearer rule architecture
- it points to specific tax and transfer mechanisms
- it is easier to describe what exactly does not fit the current asset structure

### Which is more generative?

`Private-Credit Resilience Squeeze`

Why:

- it opens a wider system-transmission picture
- it suggests concentrated wealth matters not only through rules but through the hidden plumbing of financing and withdrawal
- it creates more possible downstream sectoral comparisons

### Which currently has the stronger proving-ground value?

`US Asset-Regime and Wealth Lock-In`

Why:

- the rule mismatch is easier to show clearly with open sources
- it gives the non-climate branch a cleaner portability test of a climate-derived structure type
- it is easier to compare against the hydrologic branch's design/rule logic without overextending the analogy

### Which currently has the stronger long-run method upside?

`Private-Credit Resilience Squeeze`

Why:

- if deepened successfully, it may show how wealth concentration works as a system-transmission problem and not only as an institutional design problem
- it may also reveal a more direct path from top-level concentration to downstream resilience loss

## Working judgment

The branch does **not** fully resolve yet.

The current best read remains:

- still a `hybrid`
- with a stronger bounded `design/rule conflict` lead
- and a strong `correlation/transmission` challenger that may become more central with one more good case

That is a meaningful result.

It means the non-climate proving-ground branch has not collapsed into one of the climate-derived structure types too quickly. Instead, it is behaving like a serious test case that still needs one more discriminating comparison.

## Method implication

This comparison improves the portability claim.

Why:

- the method has not merely found analogies outside climate
- it has found a new domain where existing structure types are both relevant, but where the branch still has to work to decide which one leads

That is exactly the kind of non-trivial transfer we wanted to test.

## Next move

Do not widen the branch immediately.

Instead:

1. deepen the transmission side with one more concrete downstream sectoral or borrower case
2. then rerun this comparison
3. only after that decide whether wealth concentration remains structurally hybrid or is resolving toward one dominant type
