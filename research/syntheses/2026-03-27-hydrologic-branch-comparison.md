## Purpose

Compare the first two named hydrologic variants to determine whether the hydrologic branch now holds across different institutional forms rather than only as one good illustration.

## Cases compared

### 1. SEQ Grid and Wivenhoe Whiplash

Primary artifact: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-seq-grid-and-wivenhoe-whiplash.md`

Core mechanism: drought-security portfolio design and flood-mitigation operations strain the same integrated urban water system under rapid hydroclimatic transition.

### 2. Upper Colorado Compact and Storage Whiplash

Primary artifact: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-upper-colorado-compact-and-storage-whiplash.md`

Core mechanism: basin-scale hydrologic stress exposes tensions among allocation law, storage logic, shortage thresholds, and operating-rule redesign.

## Shared branch structure

Both cases belong in the hydrologic branch because they treat hydrologic variability as a design-and-operations problem rather than as weather alone.

Shared structure:

- inherited infrastructure and rule systems embody older assumptions about hydrologic behavior
- volatility stresses those assumptions in ways that are operationally visible
- the main risk is not only physical shortage or flood, but institutional difficulty moving across conflicting operating logics

## Main contrast

### SEQ: urban grid and dual-purpose reservoir tension

- strongest at showing a visible shift between drought-security and flood-mitigation planning worlds
- strongest at illustrating how a diversified infrastructure portfolio can still remain brittle at operating-rule boundaries
- more event-sequence oriented than the Colorado case

### Upper Colorado: compact, storage, and shortage-rule tension

- strongest at showing hydrologic design stress inside a rule-heavy basin regime
- strongest at illustrating how variability forces repeated redesign of operating assumptions
- more long-duration and governance-heavy than the SEQ case

## Evidence comparison

### Stronger on sequence visibility: SEQ

The South East Queensland case more clearly exhibits an intuitive drought-to-flood transition and therefore makes hydrologic whiplash visible quickly.

### Stronger on explicit rule architecture: Upper Colorado

The Upper Colorado case has a more explicit legal and operational framework, making design assumptions and rule stress easier to name directly.

## Method judgment

This is enough to keep the hydrologic branch active with more confidence.

Why:

- the branch now survives translation into two clearly different institutional forms
- it no longer depends on one metro water-grid case only
- it makes a stronger argument that hydrologic variability can reveal design conflict rather than just event severity

What is still unresolved:

- whether the branch is best understood as a subtype of sequence failure
- or whether it is becoming a distinct structure around `design/rule conflict under volatility`

## Branch maturity judgment

Using `docs/branch-maturity.md`, the hydrologic branch now looks like `Level 4: method-shaping`.

Why:

- it has clear framing and a distinct mechanism
- it has two meaningful named variants with different institutional forms
- it has a real internal comparison synthesis
- it has a loop-run record, stronger chronology, and explicit discard pressure
- it has now materially changed the method by helping stabilize `design/rule conflict under volatility` as a third emerging structure type

This does not mean the branch is finished. It means it now does more than populate the repo with good examples. It changes how the method names and evaluates hidden failure structures.

## Preparedness implications

- SEQ suggests stress-testing integrated urban water portfolios and dual-purpose flood/water-supply operations
- Upper Colorado suggests stress-testing allocation regimes, storage thresholds, and shortage-rule sufficiency
- together they show that hydrologic preparedness should examine infrastructure and rules together, not separately
