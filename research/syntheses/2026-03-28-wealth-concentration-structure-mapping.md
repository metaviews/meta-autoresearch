# Wealth Concentration Structure Mapping

## Metadata

- title: Wealth Concentration Structure Mapping
- date: 2026-03-28
- author: OpenCode
- status: draft
- type: comparison synthesis

## Purpose

Test whether the new wealth concentration branch maps most strongly to one of the three existing structure types already established in the method, or whether it presently looks more like a hybrid.

## Candidate mappings

### 1. Correlation/transmission failure

This is currently the strongest fit.

Why:

- the branch emphasizes hidden balance-sheet channels and intermediation rather than only visible distribution
- the BIS mechanism shows that fragility can move through under-recognized corporate credit networks rather than through obvious top-level ownership alone
- the scenario logic already treats concentration as something that changes how stress moves through the system, not just who holds what

What this would mean:

- wealth concentration is not only a stock problem
- it is a problem of how concentrated claims, asset structures, and intermediary positions transmit fragility and shape resilience across the rest of the system

### 2. Design/rule conflict under volatility

This is also a strong fit, but currently secondary.

Why:

- the grounding note already points to tax design, privatization, deregulation, and institutional architecture as part of the mechanism
- the WID evidence on housing-heavy taxation versus financial-asset-heavy top wealth suggests that inherited policy design may no longer map well onto current asset structures

What this would mean:

- part of the problem is that the governing architecture was not designed for the actual compounding structure now in place
- the branch could later become more rule-centered if grounded in a specific tax, asset, or ownership regime

### 3. Sequence failure

This is currently the weakest fit.

Why:

- the branch does not yet revolve around transition misreading or phase-to-phase interpretation failure
- the current framing is structural and cumulative rather than strongly temporal

What this would mean if it later strengthened:

- sequence failure might emerge in later work around cycles of crisis, asset repricing, policy response, and concentration lock-in
- but it is not the clearest first structure signal here

## Working judgment

The best current read is:

- `hybrid`, led by `correlation/transmission failure`
- with a strong secondary `design/rule conflict under volatility` component

That is a useful proving-ground result.

It suggests the method can travel outside climate without forcing a perfect one-to-one match with existing structures on the first pass.

## Why this matters

If the branch had mapped cleanly to only one structure immediately, that would be simpler but less informative. The hybrid result is actually methodologically valuable because it shows that non-climate domains may initially appear at the overlap of multiple structure types before deeper grounding clarifies the dominant mechanism.

## Next grounding decision

The branch should now deepen one of two directions:

### Option A: hidden intermediation and transmission

Use a more concrete corporate or financial case to test whether the branch is mainly about hidden system transmission.

### Option B: asset-regime and tax-design mismatch

Use a more concrete tax, ownership, or asset-regime case to test whether the branch is mainly about design/rule conflict.

## Recommendation

Start with `Option B`.

Why:

- it is more likely to produce a bounded, publicly documented case quickly
- it gives the non-climate proving ground a cleaner comparison with the hydrologic branch's rule-conflict logic
- it may also make later transmission questions easier to ground rather than staying abstract

## Method implication

This is the first clear sign that non-climate proving-ground branches may not begin as pure structure exemplars. They may begin as hybrids and only later resolve toward a dominant structure through grounding.

That is an important methodological insight in its own right.
