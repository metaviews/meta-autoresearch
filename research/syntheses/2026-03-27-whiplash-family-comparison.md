## Purpose

Compare the first two named variants of the `compound seasonal whiplash` family to test whether the family holds across genuinely different mechanisms rather than only across stylistic variations.

## Cases compared

### 1. Feather River Wet-to-Fire Whiplash

Primary artifact: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-feather-river-wet-to-fire-whiplash.md`

Core mechanism: a wet or recovery phase shifts institutional attention toward relief, but later hot-dry and fire-prone conditions expose the weakness of separate hazard planning categories.

### 2. Upper Colorado Recovery-to-Shortage Whiplash

Primary artifact: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-upper-colorado-recovery-to-shortage-whiplash.md`

Core mechanism: strong snowpack or drought-improvement signals are interpreted as durable recovery, but runoff conversion limits, aridity, reservoir thresholds, and governance constraints restore shortage pressure.

## Main comparison

### Shared family structure

Both scenarios belong in the same family because they share the same higher-order failure pattern:

- a visible relief signal appears
- institutions begin to think in recovery terms
- the relief signal is real but incomplete
- later conditions expose how brittle the recovery interpretation was

That structure appears robust enough to justify keeping `compound seasonal whiplash` as a meaningful parent family rather than treating the two regional cases as unrelated artifacts.

### Mechanism contrast

- Feather River emphasizes cross-hazard coordination failure among water, fuels, wildfire, infrastructure, and emergency response
- Upper Colorado emphasizes conversion failure between snowpack optimism and actual operational water security

This is a useful contrast. Feather River is more about `hazard transition`. Upper Colorado is more about `recovery interpretation failure`.

### Evidence contrast

- Feather River currently has moderate evidence support; the geography is credible, but the specific causal chain still needs tighter case chronology
- Upper Colorado currently has stronger open-access support because the whiplash mechanism is already visible in drought, snowpack, runoff, and reservoir reporting

At this stage, Upper Colorado is the stronger case analytically, while Feather River remains the more vivid challenge to default sectoral planning categories.

### Preparedness contrast

- Feather River is strongest for testing multi-hazard transition planning, public communication, and cross-system emergency coordination
- Upper Colorado is strongest for testing forecast interpretation, reservoir planning, compact governance, and the politics of apparent recovery

Together, the pair broadens the family's preparedness value. One case stresses operational transition across hazards. The other stresses institutional overconfidence during partial recovery.

## Evaluation judgment

### What this comparison says about the parent family

The whiplash family is now stronger than it was in its abstract form.

Why:

- it survives translation into two named geographies
- the two variants are meaningfully different in mechanism
- both still challenge status-quo planning categories
- both remain disciplined enough to support follow-up evidence work

This does not yet move the parent scenario fully from `revise` to `keep`. The family is now credible, but it still needs either:

- one stronger case chronology, or
- a third variant showing the pattern in another setting

### Best current variant

The best current variant for further grounding is `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-upper-colorado-recovery-to-shortage-whiplash.md`.

Why:

- the mechanism is more tightly evidenced
- the relief-to-shortage sequence is already legible in open-access reporting
- it offers a clean test of how institutions misread recovery under non-stationarity

### Most distinctive contribution

The most distinctive challenge to conventional planning remains `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-feather-river-wet-to-fire-whiplash.md` because it makes sectoral separation itself look like part of the risk.

## What this suggests about the method

- family-level comparison is more useful than polishing one regional scenario in isolation
- a scenario family becomes more trustworthy when its pattern survives across different mechanisms
- the method benefits from distinguishing `strongest-evidenced case` from `most conceptually disruptive case`

That last distinction matters for meta-autoresearch. The best-supported artifact and the most status-quo-challenging artifact are not always the same thing.

## Next move

Do not add more whiplash variants immediately.

Instead:

1. deepen the Upper Colorado case with a tighter year cluster or operator-facing evidence
2. keep Feather River active as the more cross-hazard comparison case
3. then return to the breadbasket family so the project does not overconcentrate on one scenario branch
