## Purpose

Compare the two main wheat-branch comparison variants to determine what each one adds, which is stronger now, and how the branch should proceed without collapsing trade-buffer and production-buffer logic into one story.

## Cases compared

### 1. Russia-Europe Wheat Trade Shock

Primary artifact: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-russia-europe-wheat-trade-shock.md`

Core mechanism: simultaneous stress weakens both a major export node and one of its most important substitute export buffers.

### 2. Russia-China Wheat Buffer Stress

Primary artifact: `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-russia-china-wheat-buffer-stress.md`

Core mechanism: simultaneous stress weakens a major export node while also reducing confidence in a very large domestic production-and-stock buffer.

## Shared branch structure

Both variants preserve the main breadbasket logic:

- climate correlation matters because fallback assumptions thin out
- the downstream problem is not just yield loss, but transmission through policy, stocks, trade, and procurement
- import-dependent buyers experience the shock through systems, not only through fields

That means the branch is no longer relying on one transmission pathway. It now has at least two meaningfully different ways in which correlated wheat stress can become operationally dangerous.

## Main contrast

### Russia-Europe: trade-buffer failure

- strongest at showing how export diversification can fail
- strongest at showing how a supply shock propagates through tradable substitute capacity
- most useful when the preparedness question is about reserves, tendering, pricing, and fallback exporters

### Russia-China: production-buffer failure

- strongest at showing how a large domestic system can stop acting as a hidden stabilizer
- strongest at showing how stock retention, domestic balancing, and import demand can tighten the global system indirectly
- most useful when the preparedness question is about large-buffer behavior, demand shifts, and the limits of internal stabilization

## Evidence comparison

### Stronger now: Russia-Europe

The Russia-Europe variant is stronger in the current repo because:

- the export and substitution roles are cleaner
- the trade significance is more directly supported in current open-access sources
- the preparedness implications are easier to map onto procurement and importer stress

### More provisional now: Russia-China

The Russia-China variant is still valuable, but it is more inferential because:

- its outward transmission depends on stock use, import behavior, and state response that are harder to observe directly from the current source base
- the mechanism is more indirect than the Europe trade-buffer case

That does not make it weak. It makes it a more exploratory comparison variant than the Europe case at this stage.

## Method judgment

This is a useful result for meta-autoresearch.

The branch now demonstrates that:

- one family can contain multiple mechanism types without dissolving into vagueness
- comparison can distinguish `best-evidenced variant` from `most conceptually generative variant`
- breadbasket risk should not be framed as one flat global-food story

The best-evidenced current wheat comparison is `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-russia-europe-wheat-trade-shock.md`.

The more conceptually generative but still more provisional comparison is `research/scenarios/2026-03-27-russia-china-wheat-buffer-stress.md`.

## Preparedness implications

- if the concern is trade substitution failure, prioritize the Russia-Europe branch
- if the concern is hidden buffer fragility and importer-demand tightening, prioritize the Russia-China branch
- if the concern is downstream exposure, use both branches alongside the MENA note rather than collapsing them into one scenario

## Next move

Do not add a third wheat comparison node yet.

Instead:

1. keep Russia-Europe as the best current operational variant
2. keep Russia-China as the best current buffer-comparison variant
3. use the MENA importer note to test how differently those two variants would matter downstream
