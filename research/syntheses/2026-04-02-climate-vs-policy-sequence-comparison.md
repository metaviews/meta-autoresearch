# Climate vs. Policy: Sequence Failure Comparison

**Date:** 2026-04-02  
**Type:** Comparison synthesis  
**Branch:** whiplash  
**Status:** draft

---

## Purpose

This synthesis compares climate and policy sequence failure scenarios to test whether the sequence failure structure generalizes beyond climate volatility into political economy.

**Questions this answers:**
1. Do climate and policy sequences share the same failure mechanism?
2. What domain-specific differences matter for the structure?
3. Does human agency in policy change the mechanism fundamentally?

---

## Variants Compared

| Aspect | Climate (Feather River wet-to-fire) | Policy (Regime whiplash) |
|--------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Sequence type** | Wet season → hot-dry season | Expansion → contraction regime |
| **Timescale** | Months to years | Months to years (election cycles, crises) |
| **Drivers** | Climate volatility, non-stationarity | Political change, crises, scandals |
| **Planning categories** | Flood, fire, water management | Deregulation, reregulation, compliance |
| **Adaptation lag** | Infrastructure rules lag conditions | Capital/compliance lag policy change |
| **Human agency** | Limited (climate is non-agentic) | High (policy is explicitly agentic) |
| **Predictability** | Increasingly unpredictable | Partially predictable (elections, signals) |
| **Reversibility** | Slow (infrastructure changes slowly) | Fast (policy can reverse quickly) |

---

## Shared Mechanisms (Structure Holds)

Both domains exhibit the core sequence failure mechanism:

1. **Transition is the risk** — Not the endpoints (wet or dry, expansion or contraction) but the shift between them
2. **Optimization for one regime** — Actors optimize for current conditions, creating vulnerability to change
3. **Separate planning categories** — Flood/fire or expansion/contraction are planned as independent "seasons"
4. **Adaptation lag** — Rules, capital, and expertise optimized for one regime malfunction under the other
5. **Institutional inertia** — Systems change slower than conditions, creating mismatch

**Judgment:** The sequence failure structure **holds across both domains**. Human agency does not fundamentally change the mechanism.

---

## Domain-Specific Differences (Structure Refinement)

Policy exhibits characteristics that **refine** the structure:

| Difference | Implication for Structure |
|------------|--------------------------|
| **Human agency** | Sequences can be anticipated (elections, signals) but still create whiplash |
| **Faster reversibility** | Policy can reverse quickly, creating repeated whiplash cycles |
| **Strategic behavior** | Some actors benefit from whiplash (arbitrage, political advantage) |
| **Explicit rule changes** | Policy rules are written and published (vs. implicit infrastructure rules) |

**Judgment:** Policy is not a different structure—it introduces **agency complications** that refine how sequence failure manifests.

---

## Structure Refinement: Agency Layers

Based on this comparison, the sequence failure structure has **agency layers**:

### Layer 1: Non-Agentic Sequences (Climate)
- Drivers are physical (climate, hydrology)
- Adaptation lag is infrastructural (rules, design standards)
- Predictability is declining (non-stationarity)
- No actors benefit from the sequence itself

### Layer 2: Agentic Sequences (Policy)
- Drivers are political (elections, crises, ideology)
- Adaptation lag is behavioral and capital-based (compliance, allocation)
- Predictability is partial (elections scheduled, crises not)
- Some actors may benefit from whiplash (arbitrage, political gain)

**Method implication:** The structure type should distinguish agency layers, not assume all sequences are climate-like.

## Method Lesson

**What this teaches the method:**

1. **Structure types can travel across agency boundaries** — The sequence failure mechanism holds even when drivers shift from physical (climate) to political (policy).

2. **Agency adds complexity, not different structure** — Human agency introduces strategic behavior and partial predictability, but the core mechanism (transition + lag) remains.

3. **Non-climate testing is essential for structure validation** — Without policy comparison, we might have assumed sequence failure was climate-specific.

4. **Hybrid sequences may be most revealing** — Climate policy (agentic response to non-agentic change) could test whether the structure handles mixed agency cases.

---

## Open Questions

1. **What is the limit of sequence generalization?** — Does the structure hold for technology standards (e.g., AI safety regulation whiplash), market cycles, or organizational restructurings?

2. **Does agency always complicate, or can it simplify?** — In policy, elections provide some predictability. Are there climate analogues (seasonal forecasts) that provide similar partial predictability?

3. **Should the method distinguish "pure" vs. "hybrid" sequences?** — Climate policy whiplash involves both climate volatility and policy response. Is this a distinct subtype or just a combination?
